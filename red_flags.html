Bleeding Risk — Antithrombotic Drugs
Anticoagulants · Antiplatelets · Other
Critical
⚠️
Golden rule: Thromboembolic risk of stopping a drug is almost always GREATER than the bleeding risk of continuing. Never stop antithrombotics without consulting the prescribing clinician. Use local haemostatic measures instead: sutures, tranexamic acid mouthwash (5%), gelatin sponge, bone wax.
Drug / Class
What it is & what it's for
Why it's a dental red flag
What to do
Warfarin
Coumadin, Marevan
Vitamin K antagonist
Prevents blood clots by blocking Vitamin K — the vitamin your liver needs to make clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X).
Used for: AF, mechanical heart valves, DVT/PE, stroke prevention.
Prolonged, potentially uncontrollable bleeding after extractions or surgery
Variable effect — narrow therapeutic window; affected by diet (vitamin K foods), antibiotics, other drugs
Requires INR test — no fixed dose for every patient
→Check INR within 24–72 h before any invasive procedure
→INR <4.0 = generally safe for simple extractions (per TG)
→INR 4.0–5.0 = discuss with physician; defer if possible
→Do NOT stop warfarin — liaise with GP if INR too high
→Tranexamic acid mouthwash post-op (hold for 2 min × 4/day)
DOACs
Apixaban (Eliquis), Rivaroxaban (Xarelto), Dabigatran (Pradaxa), Edoxaban
Direct oral anticoagulants
Directly block specific clotting factors — apixaban/rivaroxaban/edoxaban block Factor Xa; dabigatran blocks thrombin (Factor IIa). More predictable than warfarin.
Used for: AF (prevent stroke), DVT/PE treatment & prevention, post joint-replacement clot prevention.
Increased bleeding risk — NO routine monitoring test (no INR equivalent)
No easy reversal agent for most (idarucizumab for dabigatran only; andexanet alfa for Xa inhibitors — hospital only)
Peak effect 1–4 h after dose; trough just before next dose
→Low-bleeding-risk procedures (simple extractions): do NOT stop — time procedure at trough (just before next dose)
→High-risk procedures: consider omitting one dose — only with physician guidance
→Local haemostatic measures essential
→Do NOT prescribe NSAIDs — significantly increases bleeding risk
Antiplatelets
Aspirin, Clopidogrel (Plavix), Ticagrelor (Brilinta), Prasugrel (Effient), Dipyridamole (Persantin)
Antiplatelet agents
Prevent platelets from sticking together by blocking ADP receptors (clopidogrel, ticagrelor, prasugrel) or inhibiting thromboxane A2 (aspirin). Platelets can't form a clot plug.
Used for: prevention of heart attack, stroke, after stent placement, peripheral artery disease.
Impaired platelet plug formation → prolonged bleeding after extractions
Dual antiplatelet therapy (e.g., aspirin + clopidogrel after stent) = high bleeding risk
Stopping clopidogrel or ticagrelor within 12 months of stent → risk of stent thrombosis (life-threatening)
→Do NOT stop antiplatelets — stent thrombosis risk is catastrophic
→Low-dose aspirin alone: simple extractions safe — local haemostatic measures
→Dual antiplatelet: consult prescriber before any invasive procedure
→Avoid NSAIDs (ibuprofen blocks aspirin's antiplatelet effect)
SSRIs
Sertraline (Zoloft), Escitalopram (Lexapro), Fluoxetine (Prozac), Paroxetine
Antidepressants
Block reuptake of serotonin in the brain → more serotonin available. Serotonin is also stored in platelets and helps them aggregate.
Used for: depression, anxiety, OCD, PTSD, panic disorder.
Platelets depleted of serotonin → mild platelet dysfunction → slightly increased post-op bleeding
Bruxism — serotonin affects jaw muscle tone; may cause or worsen parafunction
Xerostomia — common side effect, increases caries risk
→Awareness only — no modification for routine extractions
→Assess for bruxism / occlusal wear clinically
→Address xerostomia: fluoride varnish, saliva substitutes, increased caries recall
→Check if patient is also on other antiplatelets — combined risk

MRONJ Risk — Antiresorptives & Antiangiogenics
Bisphosphonates · Denosumab · VEGF inhibitors · TKIs
Critical
💀
MRONJ definition (AAOMS 2014): Exposed bone / bone probeable through IO or EO fistula in maxillofacial region, persisting >8 weeks, in a patient on antiresorptive or antiangiogenic therapy, with NO history of radiotherapy to jaws and no obvious metastatic disease to jaws.
Prevention > treatment. Prioritise keeping teeth over extracting.
Drug / Class
What it is & what it's for
Why it's a dental red flag
What to do
Oral Bisphosphonates
Alendronate (Fosamax), Risedronate (Actonel), Ibandronate, Etidronate
Antiresorptive
Absorbed into bone and permanently inhibit osteoclasts (cells that break down bone). Bone remodelling is suppressed — bone cannot heal or remodel normally.
Used for: osteoporosis (prevent fractures), Paget's disease, hypercalcaemia of malignancy.
Low-moderate MRONJ risk for osteoporosis dose (oral route)
Risk increases with: duration >4 years, concurrent corticosteroids, diabetes, smoking, poor OH, denture trauma
Drug holidays are controversial for oral route — small benefit
→Optimise oral hygiene before any surgery — reduce infection risk
→Avoid extractions if possible — RCT + retain root is preferred
→If extraction unavoidable: atraumatic technique, primary closure, antibiotic cover
→Avoid implants in high-risk patients
→Follow-up at 1, 2, 4, 8 weeks post-extraction
IV Bisphosphonates
Zoledronic acid — Aclasta (osteoporosis, 1×/yr), Zometa (oncology, monthly), Pamidronate
Antiresorptive — HIGH risk
Same mechanism as oral BPs but IV administration = much higher bioavailability = much more drug deposited in bone = significantly suppressed remodelling.
Used for: cancer-related bone metastases (breast, prostate, lung, myeloma), hypercalcaemia of malignancy, osteoporosis (Aclasta only).
High MRONJ risk — especially monthly oncology dosing
Bone remains permanently altered — drug does not wash out
Patient may be immunocompromised (chemotherapy) — compound risk
→Complete all necessary extractions BEFORE commencing IV bisphosphonate therapy
→For Aclasta (osteoporosis, annual): time procedures >3 months from infusion if possible
→For Zometa (oncology): consult treating oncologist before any invasive procedure
→Refer to oral surgery for any extractions needed
Denosumab
Prolia (60 mg 6-monthly, osteoporosis), Xgeva (120 mg monthly, oncology)
RANK-L inhibitor (antiresorptive)
A monoclonal antibody that blocks RANK-L — the signal that activates osteoclasts. Without osteoclast activation, bone resorption is halted. Effect is reversible (unlike bisphosphonates) but rebounds dangerously if stopped abruptly.
Used for: osteoporosis (Prolia), bone metastases / giant cell tumours (Xgeva).
Prolia: low-moderate MRONJ risk; Xgeva: HIGH risk (oncology dose)
NEVER stop Prolia abruptly without endocrinologist guidance — rebound bone loss → vertebral fracture within months of stopping
6-monthly dosing: lowest risk window is just before next dose (end of cycle)
→Same precautions as oral bisphosphonates for Prolia
→Do NOT suggest stopping Prolia — consult GP/endocrinologist
→If invasive procedure needed: time it 4–5 months into the 6-month cycle (just before next injection)
→Xgeva patients: refer all invasive procedures to oral surgery
Antiangiogenics / TKIs
Bevacizumab (Avastin), Sunitinib (Sutent), Sorafenib, Pazopanib; also mTOR inhibitors: Everolimus, Temsirolimus
Antiangiogenic / TKI
Block blood vessel formation (angiogenesis) and cell signalling pathways that drive cancer growth. Also impair normal bone healing, which requires new blood vessel growth.
Used for: colorectal cancer, kidney cancer, liver cancer, GIST, lung cancer — IV or oral chemotherapy agents.
High MRONJ risk even without bisphosphonates — impaired angiogenesis = impaired bone healing
Often combined with bisphosphonates → compound risk
Immunocompromised state
→Consult oncologist before any invasive dental procedure
→Extractions: refer to oral maxillofacial surgery
→Focus on prevention: meticulous OH, fluoride, early caries management to avoid need for extraction

Diabetes Medications
Biguanides · Sulfonylureas · Insulin · SGLT2i · GLP-1 agonists · DPP-4 inhibitors
High
⚡
Acute chair risk = HYPOGLYCAEMIA, not hyperglycaemia. Recognise: pale, sweating, tremor, confusion, hunger, tachycardia. Treat immediately with 15–20 g fast-acting glucose (Glucojuice, 4 jelly beans, 150 mL juice). Repeat if no improvement at 15 min. Periodontitis ↔ Diabetes is bidirectional — worsening of one worsens the other.
Drug / Class
What it is & what it's for
Why it's a dental red flag
What to do
Metformin
Glucophage, Diaformin; also in Janumet (+ sitagliptin)
Biguanide
Reduces glucose production by the liver and improves insulin sensitivity. Does NOT stimulate insulin release. First-line for T2DM.
Used for: Type 2 diabetes (first-line); sometimes PCOS.
Does NOT cause hypoglycaemia alone — safest diabetic drug
Marker that patient has T2DM — ask about HbA1c, control, GP
Rare lactic acidosis risk with severe illness or IV contrast (not dental concern)
→Take as normal — no modification for dental appointments
→Ensure patient has eaten their normal meal before appointment
→Ask for HbA1c — <7% = well controlled; 7–8% = review; >8% = defer elective surgery
→Periodontitis-diabetes link: inform patient, improve perio control may improve HbA1c
Sulfonylureas
Gliclazide (Diamicron), Glimepiride (Amaryl), Glibenclamide
Sulfonylurea
Stimulate the pancreatic beta cells to release more insulin, regardless of blood glucose levels. Insulin release can happen even when blood sugar is normal or low.
Used for: Type 2 diabetes, often as add-on to metformin.
HIGH hypoglycaemia risk — stimulates insulin release regardless of glucose
Risk increased if: fasting for dental appointment, delayed meals, nausea post-procedure
→Morning appointment — patient must have eaten normally + taken medication
→Keep glucose gel / orange juice in surgery
→Short appointments; don't delay meals
→Know hypoglycaemia management protocol
Insulin
Rapid-acting: NovoRapid, Humalog, Apidra | Long-acting: Lantus, Levemir, Tresiba | Mixed: NovoMix, Mixtard
Insulin therapy
Replaces or supplements the body's own insulin. Moves glucose from the blood into cells. Different types vary in how quickly they act and how long they last.
Used for: Type 1 diabetes (all patients); Type 2 diabetes when oral agents insufficient.
Highest hypoglycaemia risk of all diabetic medications
Risk of severe hypoglycaemia if meal skipped, procedure prolonged, or patient anxious/stressed
T1DM patients: no endogenous insulin at all — more brittle control
→Morning appointment — patient must eat normal meal and take usual insulin before attending
→Ask: "When did you last eat? When did you last take insulin?"
→Have glucose available throughout appointment
→Short, stress-minimising appointments
→If hypoglycaemic episode: stop, administer glucose, do not continue treatment
SGLT2 Inhibitors
Empagliflozin (Jardiance), Dapagliflozin (Forxiga), Canagliflozin (Invokana)
SGLT2 inhibitor
Block the SGLT2 transporter in the kidney, causing excess glucose to be excreted in the urine rather than reabsorbed. Also have cardioprotective and renoprotective effects.
Used for: Type 2 diabetes; also for heart failure and chronic kidney disease.
Euglycaemic DKA risk — ketoacidosis with near-normal blood sugar; rare but serious; more risk with fasting/surgery
Excess urinary glucose → increased risk of oral Candida and genital thrush
Low hypoglycaemia risk alone
→For major surgery: liaise with GP about holding dose 3–4 days prior (per anaesthetic guidelines)
→For routine dental treatment: no modification needed
→Screen for oral candidiasis at each visit
GLP-1 Agonists
Semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy), Dulaglutide (Trulicity), Liraglutide (Victoza), Exenatide
GLP-1 receptor agonist
Mimic GLP-1, a gut hormone that stimulates insulin release, suppresses glucagon, and slows gastric emptying (food stays in stomach longer, reducing appetite).
Used for: Type 2 diabetes; increasingly for weight loss (Wegovy/Ozempic).
Delayed gastric emptying → increased aspiration risk under sedation/GA
Low hypoglycaemia risk alone
Nausea/vomiting side effect → GORD → dental erosion risk
→Routine dental treatment: no modification
→If sedation/GA planned: inform anaesthetist — fasting period may need to be extended
→Screen for erosion patterns if patient reports GI symptoms

Cardiovascular Conditions
Antibiotic prophylaxis · Post-MI/stent · Infective endocarditis · LA with adrenaline
Critical
Condition / Drug
What it is & what it's for
Why it's a dental red flag
What to do
Prosthetic heart valve / Previous IE / High-risk congenital
The restricted group requiring abx prophylaxis per TG
IE prophylaxis required
These conditions create abnormal surfaces in the heart where bacteria from dental procedures (transient bacteraemia) can colonise and form vegetations → infective endocarditis.
IE mortality: 15–25% in-hospital. High lifetime risk of IE in this group.
Dental procedures causing bacteraemia can trigger IE
Restricted group: prosthetic valve, previous IE, congenital cyanotic HD, cardiac transplant with valvulopathy, rheumatic HD in some populations
Joint replacements — routine prophylaxis NOT recommended per TG unless specific indication
→Prophylaxis: Amoxicillin 2 g PO 1 h before invasive dental procedures
→Penicillin allergic: Clindamycin 600 mg PO 1 h before
→Confirm diagnosis and prescribing history with GP (ISBAR)
→All dental treatment including scaling / RCT / extractions requires prophylaxis in this group
Recent MI / Stent / CABG / Valve surgery
Within 6 months
Post-cardiac event
After these events, cardiac tissue is healing and re-establishing blood supply. Patients are on dual antiplatelet or anticoagulant therapy, which cannot be stopped. Stress of dental procedure can trigger further cardiac events.
Per TG v4 (2025): defer elective dental treatment for 6 months post-event.
Stress-induced increase in cardiac demand → risk of another MI or stent thrombosis
Heavily anticoagulated/antiplatelet — bleeding risk
→Defer ALL elective treatment for 6 months post-event
→If pain/infection within 6 months: emergency care only — analgesia, antibiotics, drainage, temporary measures
→Consult with cardiologist before any procedure within the 6-month window

Hypertension & Antihypertensive Drugs
ACE inhibitors · ARBs · CCBs · Beta-blockers · Diuretics
High
🩺
BP thresholds to act on: Normal <120/80 · Elevated 120–129/<80 · Stage 1 HTN 130–139/80–89 · Stage 2 HTN ≥140/90 · Hypertensive crisis ≥180/120. Defer all elective treatment if BP ≥180/120 at appointment.
Drug / Class
What it is & what it's for
Why it's a dental red flag
What to do
ACE Inhibitors
Perindopril (Coversyl), Ramipril (Tritace), Enalapril, Lisinopril (Zestril), Captopril
ACE inhibitor (-pril)
Block angiotensin-converting enzyme → less angiotensin II → blood vessels relax + kidneys retain less sodium → lower BP. Also reduce strain on failing heart.
Used for: hypertension, heart failure, post-MI, diabetic nephropathy, chronic kidney disease.
Persistent dry cough (common side effect — bradykinin buildup)
Angioedema — sudden swelling of lips, tongue, throat (rare but life-threatening; more common in some ethnic groups); can be precipitated or worsened by dental treatment
Xerostomia — decreased saliva → caries risk
Combined with NSAIDs → reduced antihypertensive effect + renal risk
→Avoid long-term NSAIDs — undermines BP control and kidney protection
→If angioedema develops in chair: stop all treatment, airway assessment, call 000
→Address xerostomia: fluoride, saliva substitutes, xylitol
→Check BP at each visit — patients may have uncontrolled BP
Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)
Amlodipine (Norvasc), Nifedipine (Adalat), Felodipine, Diltiazem (Cardizem), Verapamil
CCB (-dipine, diltiazem, verapamil)
Block calcium channels in blood vessel walls and heart muscle → vessels relax → lower BP. Some (diltiazem, verapamil) also slow heart rate for arrhythmias.
Used for: hypertension, angina, some arrhythmias, Raynaud's phenomenon.
Gingival enlargement (gingival overgrowth) — especially nifedipine; also amlodipine, felodipine
Overgrowth worsened by poor plaque control; can be severe
Dry mouth, facial flushing
→Intensive OHI to minimise plaque-induced worsening of gingival enlargement
→If severe: refer to GP to discuss switching to a non-CCB antihypertensive
→Gingivectomy if functionally / aesthetically compromised and drug cannot be changed
→Overgrowth will recur if drug is not changed
Beta-Blockers
Metoprolol (Lopressor), Atenolol (Tenormin), Propranolol, Carvedilol, Bisoprolol
Beta-blocker (-olol)
Block beta-adrenergic receptors → heart beats slower and with less force → lower BP. Also prevent arrhythmias and reduce cardiac oxygen demand.
Used for: hypertension, heart failure, angina, arrhythmias, post-MI, anxiety (propranolol), migraine prevention.
Masks signs of hypoglycaemia (tachycardia, tremor) — important if diabetic patient
Non-selective beta-blockers (propranolol) can cause bronchoconstriction — avoid in asthmatics
Potentiates adrenaline vasopressor effect — use LA with adrenaline carefully; aspirate
→Avoid propranolol in asthmatic patients — use cardioselective beta-blockers (metoprolol, atenolol) are safer but still caution
→Diabetic patients: warn that they may not feel hypoglycaemia onset — check glucose before appointment
→Aspirate before injecting LA with adrenaline
ARBs
Irbesartan (Avapro), Candesartan (Atacand), Losartan (Cozaar), Valsartan (Diovan), Olmesartan, Telmisartan
Angiotensin receptor blocker (-sartan)
Block the angiotensin II receptor directly (rather than stopping its production like ACEi). Same end result: vasodilation and lower BP. Used when ACEi cause intolerable cough.
Used for: hypertension, heart failure, diabetic nephropathy, post-MI.
Lichenoid drug reactions (rare but reported — olmesartan notably) → oral ulceration, white striae
Same NSAID interaction as ACEi → reduced BP control + renal risk
Generally better tolerated than ACEi — less dry cough, less angioedema
→Avoid long-term NSAIDs
→If lichenoid reaction: refer to GP for drug review, manage symptomatically
→Check BP at visit

Immunosuppression & Corticosteroids
Prednisolone · Methotrexate · Cyclosporine · Biologics · Chemotherapy
High
Drug / Class
What it is & what it's for
Why it's a dental red flag
What to do
Corticosteroids
Prednisolone, Prednisone, Dexamethasone, Hydrocortisone
Glucocorticoid
Potent anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive agents. Suppress the immune system's inflammatory response broadly.
Used for: asthma, rheumatoid arthritis, IBD, organ transplant, autoimmune disease, allergic reactions, COPD exacerbations.
Adrenal suppression — long-term use (≥7.5 mg/day prednisolone for >3 weeks) suppresses HPA axis; patient cannot mount a cortisol response to surgical stress → adrenal crisis risk
Impaired wound healing — suppresses the inflammatory healing cascade
Increased infection risk — candidiasis, spreading dental infections
Masks pain and infection signs
Osteoporosis — often why patient is also on bisphosphonates/denosumab
→For major surgical procedures: discuss steroid supplementation (stress dosing) with GP
→For routine treatment (scaling, fillings): usually no supplementation needed if taking morning dose as usual
→Screen for oral candidiasis at every visit
→Treat infections aggressively — don't underestimate them
Methotrexate
Methtrex, Ledertrexate (weekly low-dose for autoimmune; high-dose for cancer)
Antimetabolite / DMARD
At low doses: inhibits folate metabolism to suppress inflammation and immune overactivity (immune cells are fast-dividing and need folate). At high doses: blocks DNA synthesis in cancer cells.
Used for: rheumatoid arthritis, psoriasis, psoriatic arthritis (low dose weekly); leukaemia, lymphoma (high dose).
Oral ulcers / mucositis — folate depletion affects rapidly dividing mucosal cells
NSAIDs reduce methotrexate excretion → toxicity (even low-dose) — potentially fatal
Immunosuppression → increased infection risk
Hepatotoxicity — affects drug metabolism
→Never prescribe NSAIDs (ibuprofen) — interaction causes methotrexate toxicity
→Use paracetamol only for analgesia
→Oral ulcers: folate supplementation may help (prescribed by GP); topical management
→Treat dental infections aggressively; low threshold for antibiotics
Cyclosporine
Neoral, Sandimmun
Calcineurin inhibitor / immunosuppressant
Inhibits T-lymphocyte activation → suppresses the immune response that would otherwise reject a transplanted organ or attack the body's own tissues.
Used for: organ transplant (kidney, liver, heart), severe psoriasis, rheumatoid arthritis, nephrotic syndrome.
Gingival overgrowth — one of the "gingival trio" along with CCBs and phenytoin
Severe immunosuppression — infection risk, delayed healing
Drug interactions — erythromycin, clarithromycin, fluconazole increase cyclosporine levels → toxicity
Hypertension (often also on CCBs → compound gingival overgrowth)
→Avoid erythromycin, clarithromycin, fluconazole, metronidazole (check eMIMS)
→Use amoxicillin or clindamycin as antibiotic of choice
→Intensive OH: meticulous plaque control reduces gingival overgrowth severity
→Consult transplant physician before invasive procedures

What to do
Phenytoin
Dilantin, Epanutin
Antiepileptic — sodium channel blocker
Stabilises nerve cell membranes by blocking sodium channels → prevents seizure spread. One of the oldest antiepileptic drugs.
Used for: epilepsy (tonic-clonic, partial seizures), status epilepticus, trigeminal neuralgia.
Stimulates fibroblast proliferation → excess connective tissue in gingiva
Plaque-modified — worse with poor OH
Affects ~50% of patients to some degree
→Rigorous OHI — reduces plaque-driven component
→Refer to GP: newer antiepileptics (levetiracetam, lamotrigine) do not cause gingival overgrowth

Cyclosporine
Neoral, Sandimmun
Calcineurin inhibitor
See immunosuppression section above.
Used for: organ transplant, autoimmune conditions.
Inhibits collagenase → collagen accumulates in gingival connective tissue
Worst when combined with CCBs (e.g., transplant patient on cyclosporine + amlodipine for HTN)
→Request GP review drug combination — switch CCB to ACEi/ARB where possible
→OHI, scaling, surgical correction if warranted
Calcium Channel Blockers
Nifedipine (worst), Amlodipine, Felodipine, Verapamil, Diltiazem
CCB (-dipine)
See hypertension section above.
Used for: hypertension, angina, arrhythmias.
Reduce cation influx into gingival fibroblasts → altered metabolism → excess collagen
Nifedipine most potent; amlodipine less so but still occurs
→Request GP switch to ACEi/ARB or diuretic instead
→Overgrowth improves within 3–6 months after drug change

Allergies — Penicillin, Latex, NSAIDs, LA, Sulfites
Always clarify: WHAT happened? HOW SOON after?
Critical
Allergy type
What it means
Dental action
Penicillin — Rash only (delayed, non-IgE)
Maculopapular rash hours–days after dose. Low true allergy risk. ~1% cross-reactivity with cephalosporins.
Avoid penicillins to be safe. Cephalosporins cautiously possible — discuss with GP. Usually a low-grade hypersensitivity, not anaphylaxis.
Clindamycin 300 mg 6-hrly for dental infections.
Clindamycin 600 mg 1 h before for IE prophylaxis.
Penicillin — Anaphylaxis (immediate IgE-mediated)
Urticaria, angioedema, bronchospasm, or collapse within 1 h of dose. True IgE-mediated allergy.
Avoid ALL beta-lactams (penicillins AND cephalosporins). Document prominently.
Clindamycin only for dental infections and IE prophylaxis.
Check clarithromycin / metronidazole as alternatives depending on context.
Latex allergy
IgE-mediated allergy to natural rubber latex proteins. Can cause contact dermatitis → urticaria → anaphylaxis.
Life-threatening anaphylaxis risk during dental procedures using latex gloves / rubber dam. Cross-reacts with avocado, banana, kiwi, chestnut.
Latex-free environment — gloves, rubber dam (use nitrile/vinyl).
Book as first appointment of the day (minimal latex contamination in air).
Adrenaline autoinjector on hand.
NSAID / Aspirin sensitivity
Non-IgE mechanism — blocks cyclooxygenase → excess leukotrienes → bronchoconstriction. Especially in Samter's triad (asthma + nasal polyps + NSAID sensitivity).
NSAIDs (ibuprofen, ketorolac) can trigger severe bronchospasm in sensitive patients.
Paracetamol only for analgesia — safe in this group.
Avoid ibuprofen, diclofenac, ketorolac.
Sulfite allergy
Allergy to sodium metabisulfite — the preservative in adrenaline-containing local anaesthetics (the sulfite stabilises the adrenaline).
Plain LA (without adrenaline) contains no sulfites. Sulfite allergy = allergy to the preservative, NOT to the LA itself.
Use plain LA — mepivacaine 3% (Carbocaine) or lignocaine without adrenaline.
Note: haemostasis and depth of anaesthesia will be reduced.
True LA allergy
Extremely rare (<1% of reported reactions). Most "LA allergies" are actually vasovagal (faint), adrenaline reaction (palpitations), or intravascular injection. True IgE to amide LA is almost unheard of.
Most patients reporting "allergy to the needle" or "heart racing" had a normal adrenaline reaction, not true allergy. Clarify history carefully.
Clarify the reaction in detail.
If true allergy suspected: refer to allergist for testing.
Aspirate before injecting. Use slow injection technique.

Pregnancy — Safe & Avoid Drug Guide
Safest window: 2nd trimester · Emergency care any trimester
High
Trimester
Approach
Notes
1st (Weeks 1–12)
Defer all elective treatment. Emergency care only.
Organogenesis occurring — highest teratogen risk. Nausea common. Morning sickness → dental erosion risk.
2nd (Weeks 13–28)
Safest window. Routine + necessary treatment OK.
Most stable period. Organogenesis complete. Supine position well tolerated.
3rd (Weeks 29–40)
Defer elective. Emergency care with left lateral tilt.
Supine hypotension syndrome (uterus compresses IVC). Short appointments. Avoid prolonged supine position.
✅ Generally Safe
Antibiotics: Amoxicillin, cephalexin, clindamycin, azithromycin
Analgesia: Paracetamol (all trimesters — first line)
LA: Lignocaine (lidocaine), articaine — with adrenaline OK in small doses
Fluoride: Topical — safe; systemic supplements — discuss with GP
❌ Avoid
Antibiotics: Tetracyclines (all trimesters — permanent tooth staining); Metronidazole (avoid 1st trimester — controversial); Ciprofloxacin
Analgesia: NSAIDs in 3rd trimester (premature closure of ductus arteriosus); NSAIDs 1st trimester (miscarriage risk)
LA: Prilocaine (Citanest) — methaemoglobinaemia risk
Sedation: Nitrous oxide — avoid 1st trimester; caution 2nd/3rd

What to do
Lithium
Lithicarb, Quilonum
Mood stabiliser
Stabilises mood by affecting sodium transport in nerve and muscle cells. Narrow therapeutic window — small dose increases cause toxicity.
Used for: bipolar disorder, severe depression, mania prevention.
NSAIDs reduce renal lithium excretion → lithium toxicity (confusion, tremor, seizure, cardiac arrhythmia)
Xerostomia → increased caries risk
→Absolutely no NSAIDs — use paracetamol only
→Address xerostomia and increased caries risk at every visit
Antipsychotics
Olanzapine (Zyprexa), Quetiapine (Seroquel), Risperidone (Risperdal), Haloperidol, Clozapine
Antipsychotic / dopamine antagonist
Block dopamine receptors (and serotonin receptors for atypicals) → reduce positive symptoms of psychosis (hallucinations, delusions).
Used for: schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, major depressive disorder with psychosis, delirium.
Severe xerostomia → rampant caries risk (especially clozapine)
Extrapyramidal side effects — dystonia (involuntary jaw/tongue movements), tardive dyskinesia → may affect impression taking, denture fit
Clozapine: agranulocytosis — check WBC before surgery
Postural hypotension — raise chair slowly after treatment
→High-risk caries protocol: fluoride, saliva substitutes, dietary counselling
→Raise dental chair slowly — orthostatic hypotension
→If dystonia evident: note in records; impression timing and technique adaptation
→Clozapine: request recent FBC from GP before surgical procedures
Cocaine (recreational)
—
CNS stimulant / vasoconstrictor
Blocks noradrenaline reuptake → excessive sympathetic stimulation → hypertension, tachycardia, vasoconstriction. Also blocks sodium channels (local anaesthetic effect).
Recreational stimulant — no medical indication in Australia.
Adrenaline in LA + cocaine → massive sympathomimetic effect → hypertensive crisis, MI, fatal arrhythmia
Dental erosion and perforation of nasal septum from snorting (palatal erosion)
Vasoconstriction of mucosal vessels → ischaemic ulcers
→Defer treatment 24 h after last use
→Use plain LA without adrenaline if use suspected or confirmed
→Non-judgmental history taking: "Do you use any recreational drugs?"
Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)
Omeprazole (Losec), Pantoprazole (Somac), Esomeprazole (Nexium), Lansoprazole
PPI — acid suppressant
Irreversibly block the proton pump in stomach parietal cells → reduce gastric acid production. Most potent acid suppressant available.
Used for: GORD, peptic ulcer disease, H. pylori eradication, Barrett's oesophagus, prevent GI bleeds in patients on NSAIDs/antiplatelets.
Marker that patient has GORD → dental erosion risk (regurgitation of acid)
Long-term use → hypomagnesaemia, reduced Ca/Mg absorption → potential bone health concern
If patient is on PPI + antiplatelet → GP prescribed to protect GI mucosa — do NOT prescribe separate NSAIDs
→Screen for dental erosion — palatal surfaces of upper anteriors (classic GORD pattern)
→Dietary advice: reduce acidic food/drink; don't brush immediately after reflux episode
→High-fluoride toothpaste for erosion risk management

💊 Critical Drug Interactions with Dental Drugs

NSAIDs+Warfarin
⚠️ Increased bleeding risk
NSAIDs inhibit platelet aggregation + displace warfarin from protein binding → markedly elevated INR → GI bleed, prolonged dental bleeding. Use paracetamol instead.
NSAIDs+Methotrexate
⚠️ Methotrexate toxicity
NSAIDs reduce renal methotrexate excretion → methotrexate accumulates → bone marrow suppression, severe mucositis, hepatotoxicity. Potentially fatal even at low MTX doses. Absolute contraindication.
NSAIDs+Lithium
⚠️ Lithium toxicity
NSAIDs reduce renal lithium clearance → lithium accumulates → toxicity (tremor, confusion, arrhythmia, seizures). Paracetamol is safe and should be used instead.
NSAIDs+ACEi / ARBs
⚠️ Reduced BP control + renal risk
NSAIDs antagonise the vasodilatory effect of ACEi/ARBs and reduce renal prostaglandin production → increased BP, reduced GFR, potential acute kidney injury. Short-course OK in healthy patients; avoid in elderly or CKD.
Metronidazole+Warfarin
⚠️ Significantly elevated INR
Metronidazole inhibits CYP2C9 enzyme → reduced warfarin metabolism → INR rises substantially. If metronidazole essential: reduce warfarin dose and monitor INR closely. Check eMIMS.
Metronidazole / Tinidazole+Alcohol
⚠️ Disulfiram reaction
Metronidazole inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase → acetaldehyde accumulates → flushing, nausea, vomiting, palpitations, hypotension. Warn patients: no alcohol during course AND 48 h after completing metronidazole.
Clarithromycin / Erythromycin+Cyclosporine / Statins
⚠️ Drug level toxicity
Macrolide antibiotics inhibit CYP3A4 → reduced cyclosporine metabolism → toxicity (nephrotoxicity). Same mechanism raises statin levels → rhabdomyolysis risk. Use amoxicillin or clindamycin instead for transplant patients on cyclosporine.
Adrenaline in LA+Non-selective beta-blockers (propranolol)
⚠️ Hypertensive rebound
Adrenaline causes peripheral vasoconstriction via alpha receptors. Non-selective beta-blockers block the beta-2 vasodilatory counter-response → unopposed alpha → hypertensive crisis. Use max 2 cartridges; aspirate carefully; use cardioselective beta-blockers are lower risk.

✅ Mental Checklist for Every Medication List

For every drug on the patient's medication list — ask these 5 questions
01
Will my patient bleed more than expected?
Anticoagulants (warfarin, DOACs), antiplatelets (aspirin, clopidogrel), SSRIs, liver disease, haematological disorders
02
Will bone or soft tissue heal normally?
Antiresorptives (bisphosphonates, denosumab → MRONJ), antiangiogenics, corticosteroids, immunosuppressants, radiotherapy to head/neck, smoking, uncontrolled diabetes
03
Is there an acute risk in the dental chair today?
Hypoglycaemia (insulin, sulfonylureas), hypertensive crisis (uncontrolled HTN), angina/MI risk (cardiac history), anaphylaxis (known allergies), seizure (epilepsy), syncere (postural hypotension, antipsychotics)
04
Will any drug I prescribe interact with their current medications?
NSAIDs + warfarin/MTX/lithium/ACEi; metronidazole + warfarin/alcohol; clarithromycin + cyclosporine; adrenaline + cocaine/propranolol
05
Does this condition or medication increase their risk of oral disease?
Xerostomia (SSRIs, antipsychotics, antihypertensives, antihistamines) → caries; GORD (PPIs as marker) → erosion; diabetes ↔ periodontitis; immunosuppression → candida; CCBs/phenytoin/cyclosporine → gingival overgrowth
