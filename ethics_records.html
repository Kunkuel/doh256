1Why Dental Records Matter

The Golden Rule Good records = good defence · Poor records = poor defence · No records = no defence. "If it isn't written down, it didn't happen."

Four Reasons to Keep Records

Patient Care
Enables continuity — different clinicians (and future-you) can continue treatment safely; supports communication between practitioners.
Legal Protection
A statutory obligation; the record is your defence in a complaint or claim.
Quality Improvement
Allows audit and review of care over time.
Regulatory Compliance
Required under the AHPRA code of conduct.

2What a Good Record Contains

Chief complaint — in the patient's own words (reflects accuracy and the patient's oral-health knowledge). Document how and why the problem arose, what will be done, and how to maintain it. Explain everything in the patient's language — no dental jargon. Record dental history: last visits and what was previously advised.

Abbreviations No abbreviations for common words; recognised clinical terms are acceptable (e.g. TMJ = temporomandibular joint).

3AHPRA Guidelines — 7 Elements of Good Records

AHPRA code of conduct Section 8.3 lists the elements of good record-keeping. Records should be:

ElementMeaning
Accurate, relevant, detailed, legibleClear and clinically complete
SecureProtected from loss/unauthorised access
RespectfulProfessional, non-judgemental language
Continuity of careEnable another clinician to take over
ContemporaneousRecorded at the time of the encounter

4Australian Charter of Healthcare Rights

Patients have rights to: Access · Safety · Respect · Communication · Participation · Privacy · Comment. The Charter's guiding principles emphasise access to healthcare, the best quality of care for each person, and respect for diversity.

Best Practices for Maintaining Records

Use digital systems · regular training & audits · standardisation · strong security measures. Observe the National Privacy Principles / Australian Privacy Principles (APP), required retention periods (longer for children), and patient access to records.

5Informed Consent & Treatment Options

Always Offer "Doing Nothing" When presenting options, always include the option of doing nothing. Provide all the information — pros and cons of each option — so the patient can make their own decision. That is the essence of informed consent.
