1The Chain of Infection

Infection requires six links. Infection control works by breaking any one link:

  1. Infectious agent (bacteria, virus, fungus).
  2. Reservoir (people, equipment, water lines).
  3. Portal of exit (saliva, blood, aerosols).
  4. Mode of transmission (contact, droplet, airborne, sharps).
  5. Portal of entry (mucosa, broken skin, inhalation).
  6. Susceptible host (immunocompromised, elderly).
Why dentistry is high-risk Close contact, blood + saliva exposure, sharps, and aerosol-generating procedures (handpieces, ultrasonic scalers, air/water syringe) make the dental setting a hot-spot for transmission.

2Standard Precautions

Standard precautions apply to ALL patients, at all times — you cannot tell who is infectious, so treat every patient as potentially so. They include:

  • Hand hygiene (the single most effective measure).
  • Personal protective equipment (PPE).
  • Safe handling & disposal of sharps (never recap by hand; use a rigid sharps container).
  • Reprocessing of reusable instruments (clean → sterilise).
  • Environmental cleaning & surface barriers.
  • Respiratory hygiene / cough etiquette & aerosol management.
  • Appropriate handling of waste & linen.

Transmission-based precautions are added on top of standard precautions for specific known infections (contact, droplet, airborne).

3WHO 5 Moments for Hand Hygiene

Exam Q — memorise all five Two moments protect the patient (before); three protect you & the environment (after).
#MomentProtects
1Before touching a patientPatient (from your hands' flora)
2Before a procedure / clean / aseptic taskPatient (from infection entering)
3After body-fluid exposure riskYou & environment
4After touching a patientYou & environment
5After touching a patient's surroundingsYou & environment

Use alcohol-based hand rub for routinely clean hands; wash with soap & water when hands are visibly soiled, after using the bathroom, and for spore-forming organisms. Bare below the elbows; no rings/wristwatch.

4Personal Protective Equipment

Selected by risk assessment of the task (likelihood of splash/spray/aerosol):

Gloves
Single-use; change between patients & if torn. Hand hygiene before donning & after removal.
Mask / respirator
Surgical mask for splatter; fit-tested P2/N95 for aerosol-generating procedures with airborne risk.
Eye protection
Goggles or face shield against splatter — for clinician and patient.
Gown / apron
Protects clothing & skin from contamination.

Donning order: gown → mask → eyewear → gloves. Doffing order (most-contaminated first): gloves → eyewear → gown → mask, with hand hygiene between/after.

5Instrument Reprocessing

Reusable instruments are reprocessed through a defined workflow: clean → inspect → package → sterilise → store. Cleaning (ultrasonic bath or washer-disinfector) must precede sterilisation — debris shields microbes from steam.

Classification by risk (Spaulding):

CategoryContactsReprocessing
CriticalSterile tissue / bone / bloodstream (e.g. surgical instruments, scalers)Sterilisation (steam autoclave)
Semi-criticalMucous membranes / non-intact skin (e.g. mirrors, impression trays)Sterilisation or high-level disinfection
Non-criticalIntact skin (e.g. BP cuff, chair surfaces)Cleaning ± low/intermediate-level disinfection

Steam autoclave (e.g. 134 °C) is the standard. Verify with mechanical, chemical (indicator) and biological monitoring.

6Aerosols & Environmental Control

  • High-volume evacuation (HVE) dramatically reduces aerosol spread — use during all aerosol-generating procedures.
  • Pre-procedural mouthrinse (e.g. chlorhexidine) lowers the oral microbial load.
  • Rubber dam isolates the field and reduces salivary/blood aerosol where applicable.
  • Surface barriers on hard-to-clean touch points; clean & disinfect clinical surfaces between patients.
  • Waterline maintenance to control biofilm in dental unit water lines.
