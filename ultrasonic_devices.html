1Overview & Definition

Ultrasonic scalers are dental instruments that use high-frequency mechanical vibration (~25–50 kHz) to remove plaque, calculus (tartar) and bacterial biofilm from the teeth — especially subgingivally. There are two types: piezoelectric and magnetostrictive. They are central to non-surgical periodontal therapy.

2Mode of Action

Cleaning is achieved through four overlapping mechanisms — only the first is mechanical; the rest act through the water at the tip:

① Mechanical vibration
The vibrating tip physically dislodges deposits from the tooth surface.
② Cavitation
Rapid tip oscillation creates alternating high/low pressure; where pressure drops below vapour pressure, vapour-filled microbubbles form, then implode as pressure rises — releasing localised shockwaves and high-energy microjets that disrupt biofilm.
③ Acoustic microstreaming
Shear forces in the surrounding fluid clean beyond the physical reach of the tip.
④ Water cooling / lavage
The water spray prevents overheating and flushes debris from the pocket.

3Piezoelectric vs Magnetostrictive

Piezoelectric (e.g. EMS)Magnetostrictive (e.g. Cavitron)
Energy conversionCurrent deforms quartz/ceramic crystals (piezoelectric effect), via a ceramic handpieceCurrent → magnetic field → expansion/contraction of metal (nickel-iron) stacks/sheets
Tip motion / active surfacesLinear (back-and-forth) — only the lateral sides are activeElliptical (orbital) — all surfaces active (the point is most powerful)

Heat & coolingLess heat → more efficient, needs less coolingMore heat → needs significant water cooling
Clinical feelMore precise & controlled, less bulky vibrationMore vibration / bulkier feel
NoiseGenerally quieterTypically louder

4Clinical Effectiveness & Patient Comfort

Busslinger (2002)
The piezoelectric scaler was more efficient at removing calculus but left the instrumented surface rougher.
Singh et al. (2009)
Both produce the same surface roughness, so their efficacy for creating a biologically compatible root surface is similar.
Muhney & Dechow (2010)
On average, patients prefer piezoelectric instrumentation for comfort (less awareness of discomfort & vibration).
Bottom line Both types are clinically equally effective. The choice largely comes down to personal preference — and patients tend to prefer the quieter, more comfortable piezo.

5Technique & Tip Selection

Angulation & pressure
Use 0–15° angulation and the lateral surfaces only. Feather-light "paint-brush" strokes — the force/action comes from the handpiece itself, not the operator. Only the tip should contact the tooth.
Motion & power
Keep the tip constantly moving — a stationary tip concentrates force → gouging & troughing. Use the correct power setting and avoid root gouging.
Never use the point end-on Aiming the tip point directly at the tooth concentrates energy and causes gouging, discomfort, and inadvertent cavitation of enamel. Always work with the lateral surface.

Tip selection (size-dependent)

  • Larger tips: greater energy transfer → heavy supragingival calculus.
  • Fine / slim tips: lower power, better access → subgingival debridement & periodontal pockets.
  • Motion stays linear regardless of tip size — thin tips feel less aggressive but do not become elliptical.
  • Risk with larger tips: higher amplitude + poor adaptation → root-surface damage & soft-tissue trauma.

Indications / clinical use: scaling and root debridement, periodontal maintenance, and gross debridement. Particular advantage in furcations and deep, narrow pockets (curved tips), where the small vibrating tip and fluid dynamics reach areas hand instruments cannot.

6Contraindications & Cautions

Contraindicated / high-caution
  • Pacemakers & implanted cardiac devices: older, unshielded devices are susceptible to electromagnetic interference — especially from magnetostrictive units (magnetic field) and the cable. Newer devices are less susceptible. Prefer piezo, and keep the magnetic unit tray off the patient's chest.
  • Infective endocarditis (IE) risk: ultrasonic scaling causes a transient bacteraemia by disrupting gingival tissue → high-risk patients need antibiotic prophylaxis; minimise trauma, use controlled instrumentation and strict infection control.
  • Severely immunocompromised: increased susceptibility to systemic infection from bacteraemia.
  • Severe respiratory disease (COPD, severe asthma): significant aerosol generation — use HVE.
Caution — use care or prefer hand scaling
  • Gag / swallowing difficulty: risk of aspirating water, debris, or (rarely) fractured tip fragments; procedure intolerance.
  • Demineralised enamel / early caries: increases surface roughness and removes weakened enamel → prefer hand scaling.
  • Restorations (crowns, bridges, veneers): surface scratching (especially porcelain), damage to thin "feather-edge" margins, possible cement disruption.
  • Paediatric patients: more sensitive to vibration/noise; smaller anatomy raises over-instrumentation risk → lower power.

7Dangers, Infection Control & Prevention

Potential harms & prevention

  • Root / restoration damage — prevent with correct angulation, light pressure, and reduced power near restorations and roots.
  • Soft-tissue thermal injury — prevent with adequate water coolant and continuous tip movement (most heat is generated near the base; retract the cheek when accessing molars).
  • Gingival / contact trauma — proper adaptation, avoid lateral force, and keep the metal tip off the soft tissue (gingiva/lips).
  • Iatrogenic damage to bonded lingual retainers — contact/vibration can dislodge or weaken them: adhesion is lost at the composite–wire interface (partial debonding), and rotational forces in the direction of the wire windings drive progressive debonding (Seide et al., 2022). Prevent by avoiding direct contact, using light controlled strokes, and preferring hand scaling around bonded wires. (Iatrogenic = harm caused by the treatment/clinician.)
Key principle Correct technique + adequate irrigation is the primary prevention of all ultrasonic injury — constant movement, light pressure, correct power, lateral surfaces, never the point held on one spot.

Infection control

Ultrasonic scaling is a high-risk aerosol-generating procedure (AGP). HVE (high-volume evacuation) is the single most important infection-control measure. Add a pre-procedural CHX rinse to lower the bacterial load, full PPE, and hair protection as part of aerosol-contamination control.

Prevention Most harms are prevented by correct technique — constant movement, light pressure, correct power, lateral surfaces, never holding the tip stable on one spot.

8Peri-implant Health & Disease

StatusDefining criteria
HealthNo soft-tissue inflammation (no redness/swelling/profuse BOP) and no further bone loss after initial healing. (Updated definition allows a single bleeding spot.)
MucositisInflammation with ≥2 BOP sites (or ≥1 with profuse BOP) — line/drop of bleeding within 30 s of probing — combined with no additional bone loss after initial healing. (Reversible.)
Peri-implantitisInflammation + radiographic bone loss after initial healing + increasing probing depth vs the post-prosthetic baseline. With no prior radiographs: bone level ≥3 mm + BOP + PD ≥6 mm indicates peri-implantitis.

Goals of management

Maintain healthy peri-implant tissues · minimise biofilm accumulation · ensure early detection of inflammation · educate and empower the patient for self-care.

Clinical examination

A normal metal or plastic probe is suitable. Probe with a light force (~0.25 N); peri-implant pocket depths should generally be ≤5 mm (depths can vary with how deep the implant was placed).

Risk factors

History of periodontitis · poor oral hygiene · smoking · diabetes · inadequate prosthetic design · lack of regular maintenance.

Management & maintenance

General principles across all three states: patient education (tailored OHI — interdental brushes are often superior to floss for implants; electric toothbrush & low-abrasive toothpaste), PMPR (professional mechanical plaque removal; consider air-polishing with low-abrasive glycine powder), and monitoring (PD, BOP, suppuration, and marginal bone-level radiographs).

StatusManagementMaintenance — supportive peri-implant care (SPIC)
HealthReinforce daily biofilm control (brushing + interdental cleaning); routine PMPR. Goal = prevent mucositis.Individually tailored recall — low-risk patients often 6–12 months.
MucositisMechanical biofilm removal is the cornerstone — professional PMPR plus reinforced, patient-demonstrated OH. Reversible if biofilm is controlled.More frequent SPIC until inflammation resolves, then tailored (higher-risk ≈ 3–4 months).
Peri-implantitisActive treatment (non-surgical debridement ± surgical therapy) to arrest bone loss; followed by intensive oral hygiene (see below).Every 3–4 months for the first 12 months after treatment, thereafter individually tailored.
Oral hygiene after treatment of peri-implantitis
  • Toothbrushing twice daily.
  • Interdental brush daily.
  • Individually tailored prosthesis care is recommended.
  • Oral-hygiene methods should be demonstrated by the patient to the oral-healthcare professional and periodically reinforced.

PMPR modalities & oral-hygiene adjuncts

OH as an adjunct to PMPR: self-performed effective oral hygiene alongside professional mechanical plaque removal is recommended; self-use of oral-irrigation devices with water may be considered as an adjunct.

PMPR in peri-implant mucositis — use a single mode Any one of these may be considered: plastic-coated ultrasonic tips, glycine air-polishing, titanium curettes, or chitosan brushes. It is suggested not to add air-polishing devices or diode lasers on top of conventional PMPR (curettes, ultrasonics, or both). (For ultrasonics on an implant, use the plastic-coated tip — bare metal tips scratch the titanium surface.)

In peri-implantitis, by contrast, treatment is more intensive — single and combined modalities are used, with surgical access where indicated. The "don't combine" caution above is specific to mucositis.

9Summary

  • Piezo and magnetostrictive are both effective at plaque & calculus removal.
  • Always risk-assess: patient factors (pacemaker, IE risk) and local factors (incipient caries, existing restorations, current periodontal status).
  • Use safe technique — constant movement, never holding the tip stable.
  • For implants, assess at every review prior to any PMPR; emphasise education, air-polishing devices, and interdental aids.
