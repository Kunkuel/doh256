Overview

Diagnosis Determines Treatment

Diagnosis is like a jigsaw puzzle — no single finding is sufficient. The clinician must systematically gather history, clinical findings, radiographs, and special test results to form a "probable" diagnosis before any irreversible treatment is rendered.

Classification Systems Used

  • Restorative → ICCMS 2014
  • Endodontic → AAE 2013
  • Periodontal → World Workshop 2017

Key Principle

  • No diagnosis = no irreversible treatment
  • Inconclusive findings → reassess or refer
  • History + exam + imaging + pulp tests → confirm diagnosis

Special Tests

Diagnostic Tests — Restorative

Diagnostic Imaging (2D Radiographs — Bitewing, Periapical, OPG)
Indications

Caries, recurrent caries, bone loss, subgingival calculus, periapical tissues, impacted teeth, bony abnormalities

Benefits

Quick, cost-effective, widely available, low radiation

Limitations

2D only, limited resolution, ionising radiation; follow ALADA principle

Transillumination
Indications

Cracks, fractures, caries; assessing enamel translucency

Benefits

Non-invasive, simple, quick

Limitations

Superficial only; may miss deep/small cracks; operator-dependent

Special Tests

Diagnostic Tests — Endodontic (Pulp Vitality)

Thermal Tests (Cold: Endofrost / CO₂; Heat)
Indications

Assess pulp vitality; diagnose pulpitis or necrosis

Benefits

Simple, quick, inexpensive

Limitations

Subjective; false positives/negatives; may cause discomfort

Result Interpretation
ResultInterpretation
No response Necrosis?Suggests pulp necrosis or heavily calcified/calcific metamorphosis. Correlate with EPT and radiographs.
Mild, brief response NormalNormal pulp response — pain on stimulus, resolves quickly (<1–2 sec after removal).
Exaggerated, no lingering ReversibleHyper-responsive but pain subsides promptly → reversible pulpitis. Pulp can recover if cause removed.
Pain lingers >a few seconds IrreversibleLingering pain after removal of stimulus (typically >5–10 sec) → symptomatic irreversible pulpitis. Pulp cannot recover.
Heat relieves pain Necrosis?Relief from heat or cold-induced pain relieved by heat suggests liquefactive necrosis / late-stage SIP with gas production.
Electric Pulp Test (EPT)
Indications

Pulp vitality in teeth with ambiguous symptoms

Benefits

Non-invasive, quick, easy

Limitations

Subjective; false positives/negatives; may cause discomfort

Result Interpretation
ResultInterpretation
Responds at low current Normal / VitalPulp is vital. Normal threshold varies — always compare to a contralateral control tooth.
Responds at high currentElevated threshold may suggest partial pulp degeneration or calcification; correlate clinically.
No response Necrosis?Suggests pulp necrosis. Note: EPT tests nerve fibres only — does not confirm blood supply. False negatives possible in recently traumatised teeth, calcified canals, or immature teeth.
Test Cavity
Indications

Pulp vitality when all other tests are inconclusive

Benefits

Direct pulp response; useful for heavily restored teeth

Limitations

Invasive, irreversible, causes discomfort — last resort

Result Interpretation
ResultInterpretation
Pain on drilling VitalPatient reports sensation/pain when cutting through dentin without anaesthetic → pulp is vital.
No pain on drilling Necrosis?Absence of sensation during cavity preparation → suggests pulp necrosis. Proceed with RCT planning.
Laser Doppler Flowmetry
Indications

Ambiguous symptoms or post-trauma; evaluates blood flow

Benefits

Non-invasive, objective, quantitative data

Limitations

Expensive, technique-sensitive, limited availability

Result Interpretation
ResultInterpretation
Detectable blood flow VitalPulp vasculature is intact → tooth is vital. Particularly useful post-trauma when nerve fibres may be temporarily stunned (false negative on EPT/thermal).
No detectable blood flow Necrosis?Absent pulpal circulation → pulp necrosis likely. Most objective non-invasive vitality test available.

Diagnostic Tests — Periapical Assessment

Percussion Test
Indications

Periodontal/endodontic problems — abscesses, inflammation

Benefits

Simple, quick, non-invasive

Limitations

Subjective; may cause discomfort; not conclusive alone

Result Interpretation
ResultInterpretation
No pain NormalNormal apical/periodontal tissues — no significant periapical inflammation.
Mild tendernessMay indicate early periapical inflammation or periodontal disease. Correlate with other findings.
Pain to percussion SAP / AbscessSuggests symptomatic apical periodontitis or periodontal abscess — inflammatory exudate in periapical/periodontal space. Common in irreversible pulpitis progressing apically or pulp necrosis.
Dull/flat sound on tappingMay suggest ankylosis (useful in paediatric/traumatology contexts).
Palpation
Indications

Inflammation, swelling, or tenderness in periapical/periodontal tissues

Benefits

Simple, quick, non-invasive

Limitations

Subjective; may cause discomfort; not conclusive alone

Result Interpretation
ResultInterpretation
No tenderness NormalNo significant periapical or soft tissue pathology at that site.
Tenderness / pain Periapical pathologySuggests periapical abscess, cellulitis, or significant apical periodontitis with cortical bone involvement or perforation.
Fluctuant swelling AbscessFluctuance indicates a localised abscess with pus — requires drainage. Distinguish from firm/indurated swelling (cellulitis).
Firm/indurated swellingSuggests cellulitis — diffuse spread of infection without localisation. More serious; may require systemic antibiotics.
Sinus tract (stoma) palpatedAssociated with chronic apical abscess — tracks infection to surface. Trace with gutta-percha to identify source tooth.
Biting Test (Tooth Sleuth / FracFinder)
Indications

Fractures, occlusal interferences, periodontal issues causing bite pain

Benefits

Simple, quick, non-invasive

Limitations

Subjective; may cause discomfort; not conclusive alone

Result Interpretation
ResultInterpretation
No pain NormalNo significant fracture, periapical inflammation, or occlusal issue at that cusp/site.
Pain on biting down SAP / Fracture?May indicate symptomatic apical periodontitis (periapical inflammation), a cracked cusp, or periodontal involvement. Test each cusp individually to localise the site.
Sharp pain on release Cracked toothClassic "cracked tooth syndrome" — pain on release (rebound) is highly suggestive of a crack. The flex and rebound of the crack stimulates pulp/periodontal fibres.
Pain isolates to one cuspConfirms location of crack — guides treatment decision (restoration, cuspal coverage, or RCT if pulp involved).
Periodontic

Periodontal Examination Tests

Periodontal Probing
Indications

Diagnose and monitor periodontal disease; assess treatment response

Benefits

Simple, inexpensive, widely used

Limitations

Operator-dependent; discomfort; measurement variability

Result Interpretation
ResultInterpretation
≤3 mm, no BOP HealthyPeriodontal health on an intact or reduced periodontium.
≤3 mm + BOP GingivitisGingivitis — inflammation without bone loss. Reversible with plaque control.
4–5 mm PD Stage I–IIMild–moderate periodontitis depending on CAL and radiographic bone loss. Stage I/II.
≥6 mm PD Stage III–IVAdvanced periodontitis. Stage III (≥5 mm CAL, extending to middle/apical third root) or Stage IV (tooth loss complexity factors).
Localised deep pocket at single siteConsider periodontic-endodontic lesion, root fracture, or isolated bony defect. Correlate with periapical radiograph.
Bleeding on Probing (BOP)
Indications

Assess gingival health; BOP has high negative predictive value

Benefits

Easy to perform; correlates with disease activity

Limitations

Influenced by probing force and gingival irritation

Result Interpretation
ResultInterpretation
No bleeding HealthyHigh negative predictive value — absence of BOP reliably indicates a site is stable and non-inflamed.
Bleeding present InflamedIndicates gingival inflammation at that site. Low positive predictive value — not all bleeding sites will progress, but requires monitoring and oral hygiene intervention.
Generalised BOP (>10% sites)Consistent with gingivitis or active periodontitis. Use as a treatment response marker — reducing BOP % confirms improving plaque control.
Furcation Involvement Assessment
Indications

Diagnose and stage periodontal disease in multi-rooted teeth

Benefits

Guides treatment planning; helps predict tooth prognosis

Limitations

Operator-dependent; access limitations; measurement variability

Result Interpretation (Hamp, Nyman & Lindhe Classification)
ClassFinding & Significance
Class I EarlyProbe penetrates furcation <3 mm horizontally. Bone loss present but furcation not through-and-through. Generally good prognosis with nonsurgical therapy.
Class II ModerateProbe penetrates ≥3 mm but does not pass through. Cul-de-sac defect. Prognosis more guarded; may require surgical intervention or regenerative therapy.
Class III AdvancedProbe passes completely through furcation (through-and-through). Bone loss is complete in furcation area. Poor prognosis; extraction often considered. Contributes to Stage III/IV periodontitis complexity.
Cases

Clinical Case Examples

Case 01 · Mandibular Right First Molar
HxHypersensitive to cold/sweets for months → symptoms have now subsided
SignsNo response to thermal testing; tenderness to biting; pain to percussion; diffuse radiopacities around root apices
DiagnosisPulp necrosis + Symptomatic apical periodontitis with condensing osteitis
TPNon-surgical RCT → build-up → crown. Condensing osteitis expected to partially/fully regress post-treatment.
Case 02 · Maxillary Right Second Molar (Full Gold Crown)
HxPost-crown sensitivity to hot/cold → now spontaneous discomfort
SignsEndo-Ice® pain with 12 sec lingering; percussion and palpation normal; no radiographic osseous changes
DiagnosisSymptomatic irreversible pulpitis + Normal apical tissues
TPNon-surgical RCT → permanent restoration. Adjacent premolar also diagnosed SIP + SAP (pain to percussion, cold lingering 8 sec) → RCT also required.
Case 03 · Maxillary Left First Molar (Occlusal-Mesial Caries)
HxSensitivity to sweets and cold liquids
SignsNo discomfort to biting/percussion; hyper-responsive to Endo-Ice® with no lingering pain
DiagnosisReversible pulpitis + Normal apical tissues
TPExcavate caries → permanent restoration. If pulp exposed → non-surgical RCT + crown.
Pearls

Key Clinical Pearls

Thermal Test Interpretation
ResponseLikely Diagnosis
No responsePulp necrosis (or heavily calcified canal)
Hyper-responsive, no lingeringReversible pulpitis
Pain + lingering (>few seconds)Symptomatic irreversible pulpitis
BOP Negative Predictive Value

Absence of BOP is a reliable indicator of periodontal health/stability — the high negative predictive value means no bleeding ≈ no active disease at that site.

ALADA Principle

Radiographic exposure should be As Low As Diagnostically Achievable — prescribe the minimum number and type of radiographs justified by clinical need.
