1Introduction & Epidemiology

"Caries" comes from the Latin for decay. It is the progressive destruction of tooth structure by demineralisation of enamel and dentine driven by bacterial acids. It is a multifactorial, transmissible, biofilm-mediated, diet-modulated disease — not simply "a cavity".

  • Untreated caries in permanent teeth is the most common health condition globally (GBD 2019; WHO 2023).
  • The most common chronic childhood disease in the US (CDC 2023).
  • Consequences: pain, infection, impaired eating/speaking/learning, poor school outcomes.

Why early detection matters Prevent damage before progression (avoids costly treatment) · preserve tooth structure (conservative options) · reduce pain & improve quality of life · prevent progression to pulpal/periapical infection.

2Aetiology & Pathogenesis

The caries equation — Keyes' triad + Time (Newbrun) Caries requires the simultaneous overlap of ① a susceptible host/tooth surface, ② cariogenic microflora (acidogenic & aciduric — S. mutans, lactobacilli), and ③ a fermentable substrate (sugars), acting over ④ time.

The demineralisation–remineralisation balance

Plaque bacteria ferment dietary carbohydrate → organic acids → plaque pH falls below the critical pH (~5.5 for enamel, ~6.2 for dentine/root) → mineral dissolves (demineralisation). Between sugar challenges, saliva buffers the acid, pH recovers, and ions (Ca²⁺, PO₄³⁻, F⁻) redeposit (remineralisation). Caries develops when demineralisation repeatedly outweighs remineralisation.

Ecological Plaque Hypothesis (Marsh)

Frequent sugar → sustained low pH → selects for acidogenic/aciduric species → dysbiosis of the biofilm. Disease is driven by an environmental shift, not a single pathogen — so management targets the environment (diet, fluoride, hygiene), not just the cavity.

Protective factors Saliva (flow, buffering, clearance) · fluoride (favours remineralisation, forms acid-resistant fluorapatite, is antibacterial) · reduced sugar frequency.

3Detection — Four Methods

Prerequisites for clinical detection Good lighting · a clean, plaque-free tooth · a sharp eye + correct instruments · assessment both wet and dry.
1 · Visual
Detect white spots, demineralisation, discolouration, cavitation. Magnification loupes help; correct mirror + light reflection; temporary elective tooth separation can aid proximal viewing.
2 · Tactile
Use a blunt / non-sharp probe or dental floss, with minimum force and a correct path of placement. Sharp probes can fracture an intact remineralisable surface and transmit bacteria — avoid forceful probing of incipient lesions.
3 · Radiographic
Essential. Bitewings screen proximal/interproximal caries (a vital safety net). Limitation: cannot detect early subsurface demineralisation; beware cervical burnout mimicking caries.
4 · Adjuncts
Transillumination (FOTI) · caries-detection dyes (enamel & dentine) · electronic devices (e.g. laser fluorescence / DIAGNOdent).
Caution — detection dyes Dyes stain the organic matrix of less-mineralised dentine (not bacteria) and also stain the EDJ of caries-free teeth. Removing all stained dentine can sacrifice remineralisable tissue — against Minimal Intervention Dentistry (MID).

4Diagnosis: Concept, Myths & Facts

Diagnosis is "an art and a science" — a synthesis of scientific knowledge, clinical experience, intuition and common sense. It involves deciding whether a lesion is active, progressing (rapidly/slowly), or arrested, determining its extent, and informing an individualised intervention. It forms the basis for treatment, informs the patient, and informs public-health planners about disease prevalence.

MythFact
Caries diagnosis should be based on detecting cavities.Caries is a transmissible bacterial infection & multifactorial disease — diagnosis = recognising environmental changes, not just noting cavities.
Restoring the cavity is effective treatment.Treatment requires behaviour modification and/or chemical change to correct the oral environment.
One treatment regime applies to all.Every patient's oral environment is unique — treatment must be specific to that mouth.

Caries can be classified by: site · progression · extent · number of surfaces · tissues involved.

5Zones of the Non-Cavitated (Incipient) Lesion

Exam Q — the 4 zones (deep → surface) A subsurface enamel lesion beneath an intact surface shows four histological zones.
Key difference — Non-cavitated vs Cavitated In a non-cavitated ("white spot") lesion the surface remains intact over subsurface demineralisation — reversible / remineralisable. In a cavitated lesion the surface has broken down into a cavity that traps plaque, cannot be cleaned, and is not reversible by remineralisation — it needs restorative management.

A white spot is chalky/matt when dried (loss of glossiness) and best seen after drying. DDx: fluorosis, hypomineralisation, hypoplasia.

7Lesion Types & Activity

Smooth-surface caries

Non-cavitated (white spot): earliest visible enamel sign, located where plaque accumulates, chalky/matt when dried, enamel intact. Cavitated: surface breakdown; an active cavity has soft walls/floors. Approximal: hard to see clinically, often found late as a pinkish-grey shadow through the marginal ridge — bitewings are critical; heavy probing can cause cavitation.

Infected vs Affected dentine

Infected (outer): soft, yellow-brown, "cheesy", necrotic, bacteria-laden → remove. Affected (inner): darker, leathery/firmer, demineralised but remineralisable, may show sclerotic/reactionary dentine → preserve (MID).

Pit & fissure caries

Active = white, matt; inactive = brown. On a dry tooth, chalkiness extends outside the fissure; on a wet tooth, a shadow indicates caries into dentine. Easily missed on visual exam — radiographs are an essential safety net. Main cause: patient can't clean plaque from the fissure.

Acute vs Chronic

Acute: rapid, lighter, often pulpal involvement/inflammation. Chronic: slow, darker, pain may be absent due to protective dentine/pulp mechanisms.

Arrested vs Recurrent

Arrested (remineralised): clinically intact but discoloured (brown/black), hard, non-progressive — colour from trapped organic debris & metallic ions. Recurrent (secondary): softness at a defective restoration margin; probe enters the defect without resistance → record as defective filling / recurrent carious area.

8Tooth Discolouration (DDx)

Hue
The colour family (red, blue, green).
Value
Lightness/darkness on a black-to-white scale.
Chroma
Colour saturation / intensity.
Metamerism
The same colour appearing different under different lighting.

Extrinsic (surface) staining sits on the surface (e.g. chromogenic bacteria, tea/coffee, tobacco, chlorhexidine). Intrinsic staining is within the tooth (e.g. fluorosis, tetracycline, pulp necrosis, developmental defects).
