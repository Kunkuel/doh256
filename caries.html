01 Caries Detection Methods Clinical
Visual Methods
  • Detect white spots, demineralisation, discolouration, cavitations
  • Magnification loupes helpful
  • Correct use of mirror + light reflection
  • Temporary elective tooth separation may be used
Tactile Methods
  • Use non-sharp instrument or dental floss
  • Sharp instruments → can damage incipient lesion or cause fracture
  • Correct path of placement + minimum force
Radiographs
  • Essential in diagnosis
  • Bitewings → proximal/interproximal caries screening
  • Cannot detect early subsurface demineralisation
Adjuncts
  • Caries detection dyes (enamel & dentin)
  • Trans-illumination (FOTI)
  • Electronic caries detection devices
⚠ Caries Detection Dyes — Caution
Dyes stain the organic matrix of less-mineralised dentine, not bacteria. They also stain the EDJ of caries-free teeth. Removing all dye-stained dentine may sacrifice remineralisable tissue — contrary to Minimal Intervention Dentistry (MID).
Prerequisites for Clinical Caries Detection
  • Good lighting
  • Clean tooth — free of mature plaque and debris
  • Sharp eye + correct instruments
  • Both wet and dry tooth assessment
02 Caries Diagnosis Conceptual
Definition
Diagnosis is an art and science — synthesis of scientific knowledge, clinical experience, intuition, and common sense.
What caries diagnosis involves
  • Deciding if a lesion is active, progressing (rapidly/slowly), or already arrested
  • Determining the extent of the caries
  • Informing suitable, individualised interventions
Why diagnosis matters
  • Forms the basis for treatment decisions
  • Helps inform the patient
  • Advises health service planners about disease prevalence

03 Myths & Facts Key Concepts
MythCaries diagnosis should be based on detection of cavities.
FactCaries is a transmissible bacterial infection and multifactorial disease. Diagnosis = recognising environmental changes, not just noting cavities.
MythRestoring the cavity is effective treatment of dental caries.
FactTreatment requires behaviour modification and/or chemical changes to correct changes in the oral environment.
MythOne treatment regime applies to all individuals.
FactEvery individual has a unique oral environment; treatment must be specific to the conditions in that patient's mouth.
04 Smooth Surface Caries Lesion Types
Non-Cavitated (White Spot)
  • Earliest clinically visible enamel caries sign
  • Located where plaque accumulates
  • Chalky/matt surface when dried (not glossy)
  • Demineralisation may extend to dentine — but enamel intact
  • White = increased enamel porosity; may turn brown (stain absorption)
  • Best identified after drying
  • DDx: fluorosis, hypomineralisation, hypoplasia
Cavitated
  • Visual breakdown of tooth surface
  • Active cavity → soft walls and floors
Dentine involvement zones
  • Infected dentine (outer): soft, yellow-brown, cheesy; necrotic; superficial demineralised layer
  • Affected dentine (inner): darker, leathery/hard; deep demineralised zone; may have sclerotic/reactionary dentine
Incipient Lesion — 4 Zones (from deep to surface)
1
Translucent zone — deepest; porosity ~1%
2
Dark zone — porosity 2–4%
3
Body of lesion — largest zone; porosity ~25%
4
Surface zone — relatively intact; greater remineralisation at surface
Approximal (Proximal) Caries
⚠ Warning
Approximal lesions are hard to see clinically. Usually discovered late — once progressed into dentine, visible as a pinkish-grey shadow through the marginal ridge (clean, dry tooth). Radiography (bitewings) is critical. Heavy-handed probing can cause cavitation.
  • Root surface approximal lesions need good gingival health to diagnose visually
  • Cavitated proximal lesions are likely active — plaque removal from cavity is difficult
05 Pit & Fissure Caries Lesion Types
Non-Cavitated
  • Active: white, matt surface
  • Inactive: brown
  • Dry tooth: chalkiness extends outside fissure system
  • Wet tooth: shadow → caries into underlying dentine
Cavitated
  • Micro-cavities ± greyish discolouration
  • Easily missed on visual exam alone
  • Radiographic evaluation essential — safety net
  • Main cause: patient unable to clean plaque from cavity
Clinical tip
Examine pits/fissures on a clean, plaque-free surface. Use visual, tactile, and radiographic examination together.

07 Acute vs Chronic Caries Progression
Acute
  • Rapid process
  • Lighter in colour
  • Often associated with pulp involvement/inflammation
Chronic
  • Long-standing, slower process
  • Darker in colour
  • Pain may not be a feature — protective mechanisms of dentine/pulp
08 Arrested & Recurrent Caries Progression
Arrested (Remineralised)
  • Clinically intact but discoloured — brown or black spots
  • Colour change from trapped organic debris and metallic ions within enamel
  • Hard, non-progressive
Recurrent (Secondary)
  • Softness at a defective restoration margin
  • Periodontal probe tip enters defect without resistance
  • Discoloured margin + small marginal ditch = record as defective filling / recurrent carious area
09 Tooth Discolouration DDx
Colour Terminology
Hue — the colour family (red, blue, green) · Value — lightness/darkness on a black-to-white scale · Chroma — colour saturation/intensity · Metamerism — same tooth appears different colour under different light sources
Classification of Discolouration
Intrinsic
  • Structural change in hard tissue composition/thickness
  • Chromogenic material within enamel or dentine
  • During odontogenesis or after eruption
Examples:
  • Amelogenesis / Dentinogenesis imperfecta
  • Tetracycline staining
  • Fluorosis
  • Enamel hypoplasia
  • Pulpal haemorrhagic products
  • Root resorption · Ageing
Extrinsic
  • Outside tooth — on surface or in acquired pellicle
  • Metallic: iron (black), copper (green), potassium permanganate (violet-black), silver nitrate (grey), stannous fluoride (golden-brown)
  • Non-metallic: dietary, beverages, tobacco, mouthrinses (chlorhexidine = brown stain)
  • Chromogenic bacteria (green/orange = poor OH; black/brown = good OH)
Internalised
  • Extrinsic stain incorporated within tooth after development
  • Occurs in enamel defects or porous exposed dentine
  • Routes: developmental defects, tooth wear, gingival recession, dental caries, restorative materials
Ageing changes
Teeth darken with age — secondary dentine deposition, extrinsic stain incorporation, and enamel wear exposing darker underlying dentine.
