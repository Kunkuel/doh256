Phase 1

Systemic Phase

4 Rationales

  • Recognise undiagnosed systemic disease → refer
  • Limit or modify dental treatment based on systemic findings
  • Prevent medical emergencies in the dental office
  • Prevent serious postoperative complications

ASA Classification

ClassDefinitionExamples
ASA INormal, healthy, no systemic disease—
ASA IIMild systemic disease; can walk 2 city blocks/1 flight of stairsWell-controlled DM/HTN, mild asthma, smoker, dental anxiety, pregnancy
ASA IIIModerate–severe; limits activity but not incapacitatingStable angina, post-MI, poorly controlled HTN, massive obesity
ASA IVLife-threatening; distress at restUnstable angina, liver failure, severe CHF, end-stage renal disease
Rule: ASA III–IV → medical consultation required before treatment proceeds.

Physician Consultation

  • Ask: ✓ "Is the patient medically fit for this dental treatment?"
  • Do NOT ask: ✗ "Should I do treatment or not?" — that's your decision.
CSU: Typed letter signed by student + supervisor. Written reply required before treatment proceeds. All verbal conversations documented in file.

Clinical Exam — 5 Steps

  • Overall physical health / constitutional signs
  • Oral cancer screening
  • Vital signs (temperature if febrile/infection suspected)
  • Chief concern area (thorough exam → working diagnosis)
  • Contiguous tissues (lymph nodes, muscles, opposing teeth, perio status)
Phase 3

Disease Control Phase

3 Purposes

  • Eradicate active disease and infection
  • Arrest occlusal, functional, and esthetic deterioration
  • Control causes and risk factors for future disease

Pre & Post Assessment Tools

  • Caries: ICCMS (odontogram + radiographs)
  • Periodontal: PPD, CAL, BOP, furcation, mobility, plaque index (World Workshop 2017)
  • Pulpal: Cold test, EPT, percussion (AAE 2013)
  • Occlusion: Angle's Classification

Caries Management by Risk Group

GroupManagement
Low risk, no active lesionsNo caries control protocol needed. Restore with definitive restorations. Routine maintenance only.
No lesions but moderate/high riskPlace on basic caries control protocol. Reevaluate every 6 months.
Active lesions or high riskFull caries control protocol + identify patient-specific causes (diet, saliva, fluoride, OH, medications).

Basic Caries Control Protocol

  • Professional prophylaxis
  • Oral hygiene instructions
  • Professional fluoride gel/varnish at every recall (<6 months most effective)
  • Dietary counselling — reduce sucrose/refined carb frequency
  • Daily OTC fluoride toothpaste + rinse
  • Sealants on susceptible pits and fissures
  • Restore cavitated lesions with provisional or direct restorations (NO indirect restorations at this stage)
Key additions: Xerostomia → salivary substitutes; root/smooth surface risk → high-conc fluoride varnish (22,600 ppm); high fluoride need → Neutrafluor 5000 Plus (5,000 ppm NaF); caries risk + gum chewer → xylitol gum.

Periodontal — Initial Therapy Components

  • OHI (brushing technique, interdental cleaning)
  • Scaling and root debridement
  • Elimination of iatrogenic overhangs
  • Extraction of hopeless teeth

Debridement vs Root Planing — Key Evidence

StudyFinding
Waerhaug 1956Rough surfaces → ↑ calculus/plaque accumulation
Rosenberg & Ash 1974Increased surface roughness ≠ more plaque or inflammation
Nakib et al. 1982Endotoxin only adheres to surface (no deep penetration); removed by light brushing → no need to remove cementum
Nyman et al. 1988Excellent healing with OR without cementum removal → over-instrumentation causes more harm than good
Bottom line: Goal = disrupt biofilm + remove calculus. NOT aggressive cementum removal.

McGuire & Nunn 1996 — Periodontal Prognosis

PrognosisKey Criteria

Fair~25% attachment loss and/or Class I furcation; maintainable with compliance
Poor50% attachment loss with Class II furcations; maintainable but with difficulty
Questionable>50% AL; poor crown-to-root ratio; Class II/III furcations; mobility ≥2+
HopelessInadequate attachment to maintain tooth → extract
Phase 4

Definitive / Reconstructive Phase

6 Components

  • Periodontal surgery
  • Orthodontic treatment
  • Restoration of individual teeth
  • Elective (non-acute) endodontic treatment
  • Extractions and pre-prosthetic surgery
  • Replacement of missing teeth

Periodontal Surgery — Indications

  • Residual PPD ≥ 6 mm after non-surgical therapy
  • Recurrent inflammation with PPD 4–5 mm and/or furcation-involved teeth
  • Gingival overgrowth, mucogingival defects, high frenal attachments

Periodontal Regeneration

  • Uses bone grafts, barrier membranes, biologic agents
  • Best results: deep 3-wall infrabony defects
  • Also possible in select furcation/recession defects

Restorations — Intra vs Extracoronal

IntracoronalWithin confines of tooth
Amalgam, composite, ceramic inlay
ExtracoronalCovers most/all coronal surface
Onlay, full cast crown, PFM, all-ceramic crown

Endodontic Treatment

Acute/Disease Control PhasePulp extirpation or RCT to halt disease/prevent acute flare-up
Definitive PhaseConventional RCT, retreatment, apical microsurgery

Refer to Endodontist If:

  • Calcified pulp, root dilaceration, congenital anomalies
  • Max premolar with 3 canals; mand premolar with split canal
  • Access through crown; any molar RCT
  • Retreatment; apical microsurgery; open apex
  • Root resorption; difficult patient management

Tooth Replacement — Options Summary

ScenarioOptions
Single toothImplant crown; 3-unit bridge; RPD; No replacement
Multiple teethImplant-retained (fixed or removable); Bridge; RPD; No replacement
EdentulousComplete denture; Implant-retained fixed bridge; Implant overdenture
Pre-prosthetic surgery: Remove interfering soft tissue/bone; site/ridge preservation; implant placement. All eligible patients must be informed of options.
Phase 5

Maintenance Phase

Primary Purpose

  • Ensure long-term oral health, optimum function, and favourable esthetics
  • Reassess success/failure of previous treatment; plan additional treatment if needed

Recall Frequency

Patient TypeIntervalReference
Gingivitis6–12 months—
Periodontitis (good OH)3–6 monthsLindhe & Nyman, 1975
Periodontitis (poor OH)2–3 monthsRamfjord et al., 1982
Active caries / high caries risk2–4 months—
No active disease, low risk6–12 months—
PRA Tool: Bern Periodontal Risk Assessment (Lang & Tonetti, 2003) — personalises recall intervals.

Radiographic Follow-Up in Maintenance

  • After extra-coronal or large direct-fill restorations → new periapicals
  • Teeth with selective caries removal near pulp → updated radiographs
  • Asymptomatic radiographic lesions under observation → scheduled follow-up
  • Prior active caries → bitewings every 2 years (more frequent if increased activity)

Key Evidence

Axelsson et al. 2004 — 30-year study: Structured maintenance (visits every 2 months for 2 years, then 3–12 months for 28 years) + OHI significantly reduced tooth mortality, caries, and periodontal disease. Intact tooth surfaces increased dramatically over 30 years.

Implants in Maintenance

  • Reevaluate for stability and presence of peri-mucositis or peri-implantitis

Elevated Caries Risk Indicators (= more frequent recalls)

  • Poor compliance, high plaque score, dry mouth
  • Aciduric/high fermentable carb diet
  • Immune compromise, debilitated general health
