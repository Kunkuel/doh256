1.4Pulpal Diagnoses (AAE 2008)

Normal Pulp
Normal
  • Symptom-free; mild/transient cold response ≤1–2 sec
  • No spontaneous pain; normal radiographic appearance
  • No treatment indicated
Reversible Pulpitis
Reversible
  • Pain to cold/sweet that resolves within seconds of stimulus removal
  • No spontaneous pain; no periapical radiographic changes
  • Aetiology: exposed dentin, caries, deep restorations
  • Tx: remove aetiology + restore; reassess
Symptomatic Irreversible Pulpitis (SIP)
Irreversible
  • Sharp pain to thermal; lingers ≥30 sec after stimulus
  • Spontaneous pain; may be positional (worse lying down)
  • OTC analgesics typically ineffective
  • Percussion often normal (inflammation not yet periapical)
  • Tx: RCT
Asymptomatic Irreversible Pulpitis
Irreversible
  • No clinical symptoms; responds normally to thermal testing
  • Pulp still vital but will not heal
  • Often: trauma history or deep caries likely to expose pulp
  • Tx: RCT
Pulp Necrosis
Necrosis
  • Non-responsive to pulp testing; asymptomatic
  • Does NOT cause apical periodontitis unless canal is infected
  • Caveat: calcification/trauma/no-response on all teeth may give false non-response
  • Tx: RCT
Previously Treated
Treated
  • Canals obturated with filling materials (not medicaments)
  • No response to thermal or EPT
Previously Initiated Therapy
Partial Tx
  • Partial endo: pulpotomy or pulpectomy
  • May or may not respond to pulp testing depending on level

  • Apical radiolucency present; no pain on percussion/palpation

Chronic

  • Intermittent sinus tract discharge

Reactive

1.7High-Yield Rules & Exam Traps

Must Know Symptomatic Irreversible Pulpitis (SIP) + Normal Apical Tissues is a common exam combination. SIP can have normal percussion because inflammation hasn't reached the periapex yet.
Perio Grading — Default Rule Always assume Grade B first. Shift to A (slow) or C (rapid) only with specific supporting evidence. Grade modifiers (smoking, diabetes) can only shift the grade up, never down.

Staging Upgrade Rules

  • Rule 1: Start with CAL → use RBL if CAL unavailable.
  • Rule 2: Tooth loss due to periodontitis: ≤4 teeth → Stage III; ≥5 teeth → Stage IV.
  • Rule 3: Masticatory dysfunction / tooth mobility degree ≥2 → Stage IV.
  • Rule 4: Furcation Class II or III → Stage III minimum.
  • Rule 5: <20 remaining teeth / 10 opposing pairs → Stage IV.

Sinus Tract Protocol

Insert GP cone carefully through the stoma until it stops → take radiograph to trace origin. Source is usually the offending tooth (chronic apical abscess).

Common Diagnosis Combinations

  • Reversible pulpitis + Normal apical tissues → caries removal + restoration. If pulp exposed during excavation → RCT.
  • Pulp necrosis + Symptomatic apical periodontitis + Condensing osteitis → RCT; condensing osteitis should regress post-treatment.
  • Previously treated + Symptomatic apical periodontitis → nonsurgical retreatment.
  • Pulp necrosis + Chronic apical abscess → RCT; trace sinus tract with GP cone first.

2.1Periodontium — Overview

The periodontium provides support and investment of teeth. It is composed of four tissues:

Gingiva

Soft tissue collar surrounding and protecting the tooth and alveolar bone. Divided into free gingiva, attached gingiva, and interdental papilla.

Periodontal Ligament (PDL)

Highly cellular and vascular connective tissue connecting cementum to alveolar bone. Transmits masticatory forces and supports repair.

Cementum

Thin, mineralised, avascular connective tissue covering the root surface. Provides attachment for PDL Sharpey's fibres.

Alveolar Bone

Houses and supports the teeth. The alveolar process is tooth-dependent — it resorbs following extraction.

2.2Embryology

Development Sequence

  1. Neural tube forms by ectoderm invagination — complete by 4 weeks IU
  2. Neural crest cells migrate to invade the first pharyngeal arch
  3. Oral ectoderm thickens → forms the primary epithelial band in maxilla and mandible
  4. Primary epithelial band forms two structures: dental lamina + vestibular lamina
  5. Each arch forms 10 epithelial outgrowths into ectomesenchyme (= 10 deciduous teeth)
  6. Underlying ectomesenchyme proliferates → dental lamina develops via bud, cap, and bell stages

Tooth Germ Stages

Bud Stage (~8 wks)
  • Undifferentiated epithelial cells
  • Ectomesenchyme proliferates around bud
  • Connected to oral epithelium via dental lamina
Cap Stage (~11 wks)
  • Cap morphology from uneven proliferation
  • Enamel organ = OEE + IEE
  • Stellate reticulum in centre
  • Enamel knot formation
Bell Stage (~14 wks)
  • Early: morphodifferentiation — tooth shape template
  • Late: histodifferentiation
  • Dental papilla → dentine + pulp
  • Dental follicle → cementum + PDL + bone

Tooth Root Formation

Begins at late bell stage once dentine formation reaches the cervix.

  • Hertwig's Epithelial Root Sheath (HERS) = IEE + OEE; grows apically then horizontally
  • Horizontal projection = epithelial diaphragm → outlines future apical foramen
  • Root dentine and cementum formation begin once epithelial diaphragm is established

2.3Gingiva

Gingival Development

Epithelium
  1. Enamel covered by reduced enamel epithelium (REE) after enamel formation
  2. REE unites with oral mucosa on eruption → transforms to junctional epithelium (JE)
  3. JE migrates apically → forms gingival sulcus
  4. JE settles at CEJ on enamel
Connective Tissue
  • Fibroblasts originate from perifollicular mesenchyme (mesoderm derivative)
  • Fibroblasts produce collagen → forms supra-alveolar fibre attachment

Gingival Epithelium — Three Zones

ZoneTypeKey Features
Oral Epithelium Orthokeratinised stratified squamous 4 layers: stratum basale, spinosum, granulosum, corneum. Keratinocytes = 90%. Non-keratinocytes: melanocytes (basale), Langerhans cells (spinosum), Merkel cells (rete ridge tips)
Oral Sulcular Epithelium (OSE) Non-keratinised squamous Lacks granular + corneum layers, no rete ridges, no Merkel cells, not heavily infiltrated by neutrophils. Stains + for CK4 (absent in JE)
Junctional Epithelium (JE) Non-keratinised squamous Coronally 15–30 cells thick, apically 1–3 cells thick. High turnover: every 4–6 days. Wide intercellular spaces = permeable barrier

Junctional Epithelium — Detail

Structure
  • Two zones: basal lamina (internal + external) + JE cells
  • JE cells: stratum basale (cuboidal) + stratum spinosum (flatter)
  • Cells attached to tooth via hemidesmosomes; to each other via desmosomes + gap junctions
  • Internal BL adjacent to tooth; external BL adjacent to lamina propria
  • Internal BL: sub-lamina lucida / lamina lucida / lamina densa
Function
  • Epithelial barrier against plaque
  • Allows access of GCF, inflammatory cells, and immunologic components
  • Rapid turnover → host-parasite equilibrium + rapid tissue repair
  • JE + gingival fibres = dentogingival unit
  • Numerous neutrophils present; increase with inflammation

Lamina Propria

  • Binds attached gingiva to bone; supports free gingiva; connects teeth
  • Divided into papillary layer (superficial) and reticular layer (deep)
  • Composition: ~60% type I + III collagen; ~35% vascular plexus + ground substance; ~5% fibroblasts
  • No submucosa — attached gingiva is directly bound to bone = mucoperiosteum

Gingival Fibres

Function Composed of collagen bundles classified by orientation and attachment. They brace the marginal gingiva against the tooth, withstand masticatory forces, and unite free gingiva to cementum and attached gingiva.

  1. Dento-gingival
  2. Alveolar gingival
  3. Circular
  4. Dento-periosteal
  5. Transseptal
  6. Semicircular

Anatomical Landmarks (Clinical)
  • Free gingival margin: most coronal edge of gingiva
  • Free gingival groove: shallow groove ~1.5 mm from margin corresponding to sulcus base
  • Attached gingiva: firm, stippled, bound to cementum and bone
  • Mucogingival junction: boundary between attached gingiva and alveolar mucosa
  • Interdental papilla: fills embrasure between teeth

2.4Gingival Crevicular Fluid (GCF)

Properties
  • Transudate in health; exudate in disease
  • Small quantity in health; increases in disease
  • Diffuses through basement membrane → wide intercellular spaces of JE → sulcus
Functions
  • Cleanses material from the sulcus
  • Contains plasma proteins that improve epithelial adhesion to tooth
  • Possesses antimicrobial properties
  • Exerts antibody activity to defend the gingiva

2.6Periodontal Ligament (PDL)

Highly cellular and vascular connective tissue surrounding tooth roots, joining cementum to alveolar bone.

Functions

  • Transmits forces during mastication and tooth contact
  • Contains cells that maintain and repair alveolar bone and cementum
  • Permits physiological mobility of teeth
  • Mechanoreception in control of mastication

Development

  • Begins with root formation during late bell stage
  • Dental follicle has two cell populations: dental follicle mesenchymal cells + perifollicular mesenchymal cells
  • Perifollicular cells deposit collagen and glycoproteins in the developing PDL space
  • PDL maintains undifferentiated stem cells → can differentiate into fibroblasts, osteoblasts, cementoblasts
  • Collagen fringe fibres merge across PDL width upon eruption → principal fibre bundles

Structure & Composition

Physical
  • Width: ~0.2 mm (narrows in middle third = hourglass)
  • Wavy bundles of collagen + reticular (immature) fibres
  • Predominantly type I collagen; type III coats Sharpey's fibres
  • Also: type V and XII collagen
Ground Substance
  • Unstructured material bonding fibres, cells, and fluid
  • Contains: proteoglycans, hyaluronan, lipids, minerals, water

Principal Fibre Groups

  • Principal cells = fibroblasts — metabolise ECM components; maintain PDL width
  • Osteoblasts, osteoclasts, cementoblasts (functionally associated with bone/cementum)
  • Cell rests of Malassez — remnants of HERS within the PDL
  • Undifferentiated mesenchymal cells located perivascularly — new cell source

2.7Cementum

Thin, mineralised, avascular connective tissue covering the root surface. No innervation. Pale yellow; softer and more permeable than dentine.

Composition by Weight

  • 65% inorganic (hydroxyapatite)
  • 23% organic (~90% type I collagen; also type III, XII)
  • 12% water

Cemento-Enamel Junction (CEJ)

Three relationships found (Nanci 2007):

  • 60–65% — cementum overlaps enamel (most common)
  • 30% — cementum forms a butt-joint
  • 5–10% — cementum falls short of enamel (dentine exposed) (hypersensitivity risk)

2.8Alveolar Bone

Development

  • Linked to PDL and cementum development — late bell stage
  • Forms from osteoblasts differentiated from dental follicle ectomesenchyme
  • Tooth germs and alveolar bone grow to accommodate each other
  • Bone structure determined by size and shape of developing roots
  • Dental follicle cells simultaneously differentiate into fibroblasts and cementoblasts

Structure

Cortical Plates
  • Dense outer bone surfaces
  • Functional unit = osteon
  • Haversian canals (parallel), Volkmann's canals (perpendicular)
  • Lamellae concentrically arranged
  • Osteocytes in lacunae connected via canaliculi
Alveolar Bone Proper (Bundle Bone)
  • Also: cribriform plate
  • 0.1–0.4 mm thick
  • Contains Volkmann's canals for vessels + nerves
  • Provides Sharpey's fibre attachment
  • Radiographically = lamina dura
Spongiosa (Trabecular Bone)
  • Cancellous bone; lamellae not in osteons
  • Trabeculae along lines of stress
  • Bone marrow: red (haematopoietic) + yellow (fatty)

Composition

Organic Matrix (tensile strength)
  • Cells: mesenchymal stem cells, osteoblasts, osteocytes, osteoclasts
  • ~90% type I collagen; small amounts type III, V
Inorganic Matrix (compressive strength)
  • 85% hydroxyapatite
  • 10% calcium carbonate
  • Trace: fluoride, potassium, magnesium

Periosteum & Endosteum

Periosteum (external surface)
  • Inner layer: loosely arranged osteogenic layer; osteoprogenitor cells
  • Outer layer: dense, vascular, fibrous; fibroblasts
  • Attaches via Sharpey's fibres
  • Roles: fracture repair, bone nourishment, nociception
Endosteum (internal surface)
  • Single layer of osteoblasts ± small amount of CT
  • Attaches to bone via Sharpey's fibres

Blood & Nerve Supply

Blood Supply

Main arteries → maxilla: anterior/posterior alveolar, infra-orbital, nasopalatine, greater palatine. Mandible: inferior alveolar, sublingual, mental, buccal/facial.

Terminal supply to gingiva/bone via: PDL vessels, arterioles, and supraperiosteal vessels.

Nerve Supply

From trigeminal nerve (V2 maxillary, V3 mandibular). Fibres enter via Volkmann's canals → PDL space → travel apically and gingivally.

Free nerve endings extend to dentogingival junction and between epithelial cells.

3.3Discolouration / Surface Staining

Extrinsic (Surface) Staining

Located outside tooth substance, on the tooth surface or acquired pellicle (Watts & Addy, 2001).

  • Metallic: Iron supplements, stannous fluoride, chlorhexidine (CHX) mouthrinses.
  • Non-metallic: Tobacco (smoking/chewing), food and drink.

Intrinsic Staining

Stains incorporated into enamel or dentine.

  • Developmental: Amelogenesis imperfecta, dentinogenesis imperfecta.
  • Acquired: Caries, endodontics, restorative materials, tooth wear, recession.

3.4Acquisition of Oral Micro-organisms

  • Within hours of birth, the sterile oral cavity is colonised by facultative and aerobic bacteria resembling the mother's vaginal canal (or skin for C-sections).
  • By day 2, anaerobic bacteria can be detected in the infant's edentulous mouth.
  • Streptococcus salivarius and S. mitis are the first dominant colonisers, alongside Veillonella, Neisseria, Actinomyces, and Staphylococcus spp.
  • After tooth eruption, more complex microbiota establish, including S. sanguinis, Lactobacillus spp., and S. oralis.
  • Bacterial diversity continues to increase with more teeth erupting.

Six Major Oral Ecological Niches

  1. Supragingival hard surfaces (teeth, implants, restorations, prostheses)
  2. Subgingival regions / periodontal/peri-implant pocket
  3. Buccal, palatal epithelium and floor of the mouth
  4. Dorsum of the tongue
  5. Tonsils
  6. Saliva
Transmission vs Contagion Transmission of bacteria between individuals is possible (e.g., kissing), but this is not the same as contagious. Little evidence that periodontal infections are communicable because they are host-mediated diseases.

3.5Dental Plaque as a Biofilm

Biofilm (Costerton et al. 1994): A co-operative ecosystem of matrix-enclosed microbial communities adherent to each other and/or to surfaces or interfaces.

Dental plaque (Marsh & Bradshaw, 1995): A diverse microbial community found on the tooth surface embedded in a matrix of polymers of bacterial and salivary origin.

Three Components of All Biofilms (Socransky & Haffajee, 2005)

  1. A surface for attachment
  2. The biofilm itself
  3. Bulk fluid passing over the biofilm (nutrients, waste, transport of cells)

Key Characteristics of Biofilms

  1. Complex, dynamic community structures: Bacterial cells (15–20% by volume) in a glycocalyx matrix (75–80%). Water channels permit nutrient passage.
  2. Altered gene expression: Biofilm bacteria express different genes than planktonic bacteria. Genes may be upregulated or downregulated, contributing to antibiotic resistance.
  3. Intercellular communication (Quorum Sensing): Chemical signals accumulate within the biofilm to sufficient concentrations to alter gene expression — e.g., bacteria delay toxin production until population is large enough to survive host defences.
  4. Reduced susceptibility to antimicrobials via four mechanisms (Stewart & Costerton, 2001):
    • Altered microenvironment — bacteria enter quiescent (non-growing) state
    • Effective stress response — some cells sacrificed to protect others
    • Slow penetration — glycocalyx binds and restricts antimicrobials
    • Higher frequency of persister cell formation — dormant cells accumulate

3.6Mechanisms of Dental Plaque Biofilm Formation

Six sequential stages (Marsh & Bradshaw, 1995):

  1. Formation of the acquired pellicle — forms immediately after cleaning; <1 µm thick; plateau reached in ~90–120 min. Contains host molecules (mucins, antibodies, amylase, statherin, lysozyme) and bacterial molecules.
  2. Non-specific reversible attachment — physico-chemical interactions between salivary bacteria and pellicle. Primary colonisers include Streptococci and Actinomyces spp.
  3. Specific irreversible attachment — short-range stereo-chemical interactions via bacterial adhesins (fibrils on streptococci; fimbriae on Actinomyces) to pellicle receptor molecules.
  4. Co-aggregation — secondary colonisers attach to primary colonisers via adhesin-receptor pairs. Fusobacterium acts as a bridge between early and late colonisers.
  5. Stratification — horizontal and vertical stratification creates microenvironments (varying pH, O₂ tension, nutrients), allowing diverse, otherwise incompatible species to coexist.
  6. Climax community formation — stable community with relatively constant composition; sub-gingival plaque influenced by gingival crevicular fluid (GCF).

  • Symbiosis: Homeostatic relationship between microbiome and host — compatible with health.
  • Dysbiosis: Imbalance between microbiome and host — capable of inducing disease.

Clinical Pearl The orange complex is rarely absent when the red complex is present. Red complex bacteria are found at low levels in health but increase dramatically in gingivitis and periodontitis.

3.9Dental Calculus

Dental calculus = calcified bacterial biomass with an adherent unmineralised biofilm on its surface.

Supragingival Calculus
  • Creamy-white to dark yellow/brown; moderate hardness
  • Forms adjacent to salivary gland ducts (lingual mandibular anteriors; buccal maxillary first molars)
  • Mineralised by saliva
Subgingival Calculus
  • Brown-black; hard; found in most periodontal pockets
  • Firmly attached from CEJ to near the base of the pocket
  • Mineralised by GCF in the presence of inflammatory exudate
  • Detected tactilely, radiographically, or visually upon retraction

Composition

  • Inorganic: Supra ≈ 37% mineral; Sub ≈ 58% mineral. Crystals include OCP, HAP, WHT (whitlockite), DCPD (brushite).
  • Organic: Protein-carbohydrate complexes, lipids, bacteria, epithelial cells, immune cells.

Attachment to Tooth Surface (Selvig, 1970)

  1. Via organic pellicle on cementum, enamel or dentine
  2. Mechanical locking into surface irregularities (resorption lacunae)
  3. Close adaptation to depressions/mounds on intact cementum
  4. Penetration into cementum

Formation Timeline

  • Always preceded by bacterial biofilm formation
  • Calcification begins in 4–8 hours
  • 50% mineralised by 2 days; 60–90% by 12 days

Hypotheses for Calculus Formation

  1. Loss of CO₂: pH rise → calcium salt precipitation from supersaturated saliva.
  2. Colloidal precipitation: Salivary proteins increase viscosity → matrix for inorganic salt precipitation.
  3. Enzymatic: Phosphatase hydrolyses organic phosphates → phosphate ions → calcium phosphate precipitation.
  4. Epitactic (heterogenous nucleation): Seeding by calcium phosphate-like agents → crystallisation and coalescence.
Aetiological Significance Calculus is the secondary aetiological agent. The unmineralised plaque on its surface is the primary agent. When plaque is removed from a calculus surface, disease does not occur (Allen & Kerr 1965; Listgarten & Ellegaard 1973; Nyman et al. 1988).

3.10Summary / Key Conclusions

  • Tooth deposits are classified as soft (plaque, materia alba, food remnants), hard (calculus), or discolouration.
  • The oral cavity is colonised within hours of birth; microbial diversity increases throughout life across six ecological niches.
  • Dental plaque is a biofilm with staged formation and site-specific composition.
  • Current evidence supports the Polymicrobial Synergy and Dysbiosis (PSD) model for periodontitis aetiology.
  • Red complex bacteria (P. gingivalis, T. denticola, T. forsythia) are most closely associated with clinical disease.
  • Dental plaque is the primary aetiological agent; calculus is secondary (provides surface for plaque accumulation).

5.3The Classic Four-Stage Model (Page & Schröder 1976)

Periodontal disease is not a single event, but a time-dependent sequence of tissue changes in response to bacterial plaque.

Critical Caveat The histological stages do not correlate cleanly with clinical signs and symptoms. Tissue changes can precede or lag clinical presentation.

1. Initial Lesion (0–4 days)

  • Very localised lesion: inflammatory infiltrate confined to ~5–10% of JE connective tissue.
  • Epithelial changes: proliferation of junctional epithelium; widened intercellular spaces; reduced cell cohesion → reduced stability and increased permeability.
  • Connective tissue: largely intact, dense and well-organised collagen fibres; no significant tissue destruction.
  • Immune cells: small numbers of migrating leukocytes — mainly PMNs, with lymphocytes, macrophages, and occasional plasma cells.
  • Early immune activity: presence of IgG indicates early adaptive immune involvement.
  • Early structural change: initial perivascular collagen breakdown may begin.
  • Overall: subclinical, early inflammatory stage with limited spread and no overt tissue damage or attachment loss.

2. Early Lesion (4–7 days)

  • Expansion of lesion: inflammatory infiltrate extends to ~5–15% of connective tissue.
  • Immune shift: dense lymphoid infiltrate dominates — T and B lymphocytes make up ~75% of cells → transition toward adaptive immunity.
  • Cell distribution:
    • ICT (inflammatory CT): immunoblasts present at lateral and apical regions (active immune activation).
    • JE: PMNs + lymphocytes maintain defence against plaque biofilm.
  • Tissue destruction: significant collagen loss (~60–70%) indicating early structural breakdown.
  • Fibroblast changes: altered fibroblast function → reduced repair capacity and increased net tissue degradation.
  • Overall: active immune expansion with substantial CT breakdown but still a reversible disease stage.

3. Established Lesion (2–3 weeks)

  • Immune profile: plasma cells dominate → established adaptive (humoral) response.
  • Perivascular inflammation: immune activity centred around blood vessels (perivascular CT infiltrate).
  • Immunoglobulins: IgG predominant, some IgA; IgM largely absent (no early-phase response).
  • Epithelial change: apical migration of junctional epithelium → pocket formation.
  • Vascular changes: increased vascular proliferation (angiogenesis) supporting chronic inflammation.
  • Connective tissue: collagen loss with early fibrosis/scarring.
  • Clinical status: potentially reversible but a high-risk stage for progression to periodontitis.

4. Advanced Lesion (no defined onset)

  • Represents long-standing chronic disease.
  • Lesion progression: extension of inflammation into deeper periodontal structures.
  • Immune profile: dense leukocyte infiltrate with plasma cell predominance and degeneration (end-stage chronic inflammation).
  • Hard tissue loss: alveolar bone destruction → clinical attachment loss.
  • Soft tissue response: dense fibrosis in surrounding CT (attempted containment / wound-like repair).
  • Overall: irreversible destructive stage of periodontitis with ongoing inflammation, structural breakdown, and tissue remodelling.

5.4Early Inflammatory Changes

Vascular

  • Increased microvasculature and vasodilation (Lindhe & Rylander 1975).
  • Vessels become more irritable and fragile → erythema and blood on probing (Egelberg 1967).

Structural

Infiltration of CT by leukocytes

  • Brecx et al. 1988: ~20% of connective tissue infiltrated at 21 days.
  • Schröder 1970: distribution is not uniform
    • Coronally: 2–64% affected
    • Apically: 0–12% affected

Changes in junctional epithelium

  • Schröder 1970: epithelial cells become flattened and lack junctional continuity.

Molecular

Adhesion Molecules — Moughal et al. 1992
  • Early gingival inflammation driven by increased adhesion molecule expression: ELAM-1 and ICAM-1 in JE and oral epithelium.
  • These molecules enable leukocyte recruitment and attachment, especially coronally.
  • Despite activation of recruitment pathways, no significant net increase in T-cells or PMNs yet.
  • Key idea: tissue is "primed" for inflammation before major cellular infiltration occurs.
Cytokine Activation — Kinane et al. 1991
  • IL-1 peaks within ~7 days of plaque accumulation.
  • IL-1 drives upregulation of ELAM-1 and ICAM-1 → coordinates leukocyte recruitment.
  • Adhesion molecules also peak ~7 days, aligning with cytokine activity.
  • Key idea: IL-1 acts as an early master regulator initiating the inflammatory cascade.
Mediator Summary
  • ELAM-1: mediates early leukocyte "rolling" and initial adhesion to epithelial/endothelial surfaces.
  • ICAM-1: promotes firm leukocyte adhesion and transmigration into gingival tissue.
  • IL-1: key pro-inflammatory cytokine that initiates and amplifies inflammation by upregulating adhesion molecules and driving leukocyte recruitment and tissue breakdown.

Cellular

Predominance of PMNs (first line of defence)

  • Innate immune response — PMNs make up 95–98% of inflammatory cells (Attström 1971).
  • ~4× increase in PMNs (Lindhe & Rylander 1975).
  • Rapid migration to phagocytose bacteria and limit plaque invasion.

Location of PMNs

  • Confined beneath the junctional epithelium — the initial bacterial interface zone.
  • The body attempts to contain bacteria at the epithelial barrier.

5.5Late Inflammatory Changes

Vascular

  • Development of dense capillary loop networks.

Perivascular Aggregation

  • Plasma cells cluster around blood vessels.
  • Enables ongoing immune cell recruitment and antibody production locally.
  • Vessels become immune cell staging grounds.

Different Stages of Differentiation

  • Mixed population of immune cells — some newly formed, some mature.
  • Indicates sustained inflammation: a dynamic but chronically inflamed state.

Spatial Distribution

  • Young lesions: plasma cells mainly perivascular, localised and vascular-centred.
  • Old established lesions: extend towards the sulcular epithelium → spread of inflammation towards tooth and pocket.

5.6Modern Overview of Progression

The classic four-stage model overlaid with modern immunopathogenesis concepts (Ohlrich et al. 2009).

Initial Lesion

Subclinical, early inflammatory response to plaque — activation of innate immunity and vascular changes before clinical signs are visible. Begins within 0–4 days.

  • Vascular changes: increased permeability → oedema and ↑ gingival crevicular fluid (GCF).
  • Innate immune activation: predominance of PMNs (neutrophils) migrating into junctional epithelium.
  • Inflammatory amplification: activation of complement system and mast cells (histamine, TNF-α) → vasodilation and further leukocyte recruitment.
  • Adhesion signalling: upregulation of ELAM-1 and ICAM-1 enables leukocyte migration into tissue.
  • Tissue effects: early CT breakdown due to bacterial enzymes and host inflammatory mediators.
  • PMN function: ineffective bacterial clearance via abortive phagocytosis and NETosis → contributes to collateral tissue damage.

Early Lesion

Transition from innate to adaptive immune response with ongoing epithelial and vascular activation, but still no irreversible destruction.

  • Immune shift: increasing lymphocytes (~70%) and macrophages → adaptive immunity becoming dominant.
  • Vascular changes: activation of dormant capillary beds + increased permeability.
  • Epithelium: widened intercellular spaces → weakened barrier.
  • Clinical effect: ↑ GCF (subclinical inflammation becomes measurable).
  • Adhesion molecules: ↑ ELAM-1 and ICAM-1 → leukocyte recruitment amplified.
  • Neutrophils: ~4× increase in JE → persistent innate defence.
  • Cytokines: epithelial IL-8 production → strong neutrophil/NET chemotaxis signal.
  • Immune profile: perivascular lymphocyte/macrophage infiltrate, CD4 : CD8 ≈ 2 : 1.
  • Interpretation: resembles type IV delayed hypersensitivity reaction.
  • Key point: no attachment loss yet — reversible stage.

Established Lesion

Chronic, B-cell / plasma cell–dominated lesion with structural pocket formation beginning.

  • Immune shift: B cells and plasma cells dominate (chronic humoral response).
  • Cytokines: IL-1 and IL-6 predominance → sustained inflammation and tissue breakdown signalling.
  • Epithelial change: apical migration of junctional epithelium → periodontal pocket formation.
  • Timeframe: variable/uncertain (depends on plaque persistence).
  • Interpretation: stable chronic gingivitis or early periodontitis boundary stage.

Advanced Lesion

Irreversible periodontal destruction with tissue breakdown and attempted containment.

  • Key feature: significant attachment loss (diagnostic of periodontitis).
  • Enzymes: matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) → collagen and extracellular matrix destruction.
  • Cytokines: persistent inflammatory signalling activates fibroblasts + macrophages → destructive cycle.
  • Tissue response: formation of a fibrous, relatively non-infiltrated band attempting to wall off the lesion.
  • Interpretation: chronic destructive inflammation with failed repair and structural breakdown.
Overall Concept Periodontal disease as a progressive immunological and structural transition:
  • Early lesion → lymphocyte-driven immune activation (reversible)
  • Established lesion → plasma cell–dominant chronic inflammation + pocket formation
  • Advanced lesion → MMP-driven CT + bone destruction (irreversible)

Periodontal progression reflects a shift from T-cell–mediated early inflammation → plasma-cell–driven chronic response → MMP/cytokine-driven CT destruction.

6.1Introduction

Primary Disease Factor Plaque is the primary disease factor for periodontal disease.
  • The aim of oral hygiene practices is the disruption of oral biofilm.
  • Gingivitis typically develops within 10–14 days in the absence of oral hygiene practices, but will resolve when recommenced (Löe et al. 1965).
  • Dental caries is a diet-related disease also associated with the accumulation of dental biofilm.
  • Major oral diseases may be prevented via thorough plaque removal, achieved using oral hygiene procedures.
  • Clinicians should be familiar with the oral care market.

6.2Prevention of Pathogenic Bacterial Accumulation (Supra/Subgingival)

  • Waerhaug (1981): toothbrush bristles can penetrate up to 0.9 mm below the gingival crevice.

Deep Pockets (>6 mm)

Earlier studies: Cercek et al. 1983, Kho et al. 1985, Beltram et al. 1987, Loos et al. 1988

In deep periodontal pockets, a reduction in supragingival plaque may not significantly influence subgingival plaque. Therefore, supragingival cleaning alone is insufficient — it must be combined with professional instrumentation to provide significant clinical improvement.

Shallow Pockets (<6 mm)

Later studies: Dahlen et al. 1992, Katsanoulas et al. 1992, McNabb et al. 1992, Haffajee et al. 2001 (moderately deep sites)

In shallow pockets, meticulous oral hygiene can alter the subgingival microflora and consequently the periodontal condition.

6.3Maintenance of Healthy Clinical Parameters

Axelsson & Lindhe (1978) — Pioneering Study

  • Long-term effects of repeated OHI and regular prophylaxis (every 2–3 months) in preventing periodontal disease and caries.
  • People (young, adults, old) who used proper OH techniques over a 3-year period had negligible signs of gingivitis, no attachment loss, and rarely developed carious lesions.
  • In contrast, age-matched individuals receiving only symptomatic treatment and no formal OHI suffered gingivitis, attachment loss, and developed new and recurrent caries.
  • Conclusion: bacterial plaque has a key role in aetiology/pathogenesis of periodontal disease, and a prophylactic program based on mechanical plaque control may successfully prevent it.

Follow-up Studies — Axelsson et al. (1991, 2004)

  • 15-year (1991) and 30-year (2004) follow-ups.
  • Recall intervals extended from 2–3 months (first 6 years) to once or twice a year for most of the group.
  • Outcomes: low incidence of caries and tooth mortality, and almost no additional attachment loss.
Key Finding Regular professional cleaning + consistent high standard of OH (>80% surfaces plaque free) minimises recurrence of caries and periodontitis. Teeth that consistently demonstrated a plaque score <20% corresponded with long-term, stable periodontal health.

6.4Prevention of Radiographic Bone Loss

Schei et al. (1959)

  • Relationship between proficiency of OH and alveolar bone loss in 737 male workers in Oslo.
  • Subjects with "not good" plaque control had greater alveolar bone loss compared to "fairly good" and "good" groups.
Take-home Loss of alveolar bone increases with decreasing efficiency of oral hygiene.

6.5Toothbrushing: Manual vs Powered

Electric/powered toothbrushes are commercially available in several actions: circular, rotation-oscillation, counter-oscillation, sonic, ultrasonic, and ionic. Features that help: bristles going in different directions, soft bristles, smaller head.

Rotation-oscillation: head rotates in one direction, then the other (e.g., Braun Oral-B Plak). Resembles professionally used rotatory instruments.

Boyd et al. (1989) / Murray et al. (1989)

  • Electric TB equally as effective as the combination of conventional TB, floss, and toothpicks in controlling gingivitis and decreasing potential pathogenic microflora.

Van der Weijden et al. (1994)

  • Braun Plak Control (rotating-oscillating) = more effective vs manual TB.
  • More effective at cleaning the lingual interproximal and lingual surfaces.

Haffajee et al. (2001)

  • Electric TB (rotating-oscillating) significantly decreases BOP (82% vs 69%) and plaque index (77% vs 65%).
  • Greatest benefit = mandibular lingual surfaces.

Niederman (2003) — Cochrane Review

  • Meta-analysis: some powered toothbrushes with a rotation-oscillation action achieve a significant, but modest, reduction in plaque and gingivitis vs manual toothbrushes.

Deacon et al. (2010) — Cochrane Systematic Review

  • Only rotation-oscillation ETB reduced plaque and gingivitis significantly (11% and 6% respectively) in the short term; gingivitis decreased significantly by 17% over 3 months.
  • Differences were small; clinical importance unclear (study duration too short).
  • Other toothbrushes did not yield statistically significant differences.

Van der Weijden et al. (2011) — Systematic Review

  • Oscillating-rotating toothbrushes are safe compared to manual toothbrushes and do not pose a clinically relevant concern to hard or soft tissues.

6.6Toothbrushing: Frequency, Force, Duration

Frequency

  • 2× daily activity advised.
  • However, literature shows it takes longer than 12 hours for gingivitis to develop:
    • Löe et al. (1965): 10–21 days for gingivitis to develop.
    • Lang et al. (1973): effective OH at intervals of 48 hours was compatible with gingival health.
  • The average person is not deemed an effective brusher (Westfelt 1996).
  • De la Rosa et al. (1979): can leave up to 60% plaque after supervised brushing.
  • Morris et al. (2001) (1998 Adult Dental Health Survey): mean proportion of teeth with plaque deposits 30–44% after toothbrushing.
  • 'Modified Bass technique' with soft bristled toothbrush.
  • Multi-level bristle tufts and angled bristle tufts are better vs traditional flat-trim configuration (Slots et al. 2012 review).

Force

Van der Weijden (1998) 400 g of force is the optimal level for a manual toothbrush. Forces above this become less effective — likely related to brush filaments becoming splayed.

Duration

Van der Weijden et al. (1993)

  • Investigated ideal brushing times using 3 ETBs and 1 manual.
  • Efficacy of all 4 brushes increased with time; the major part of the effect was reached at 30 seconds per quadrant (i.e. 2 minutes total).
  • Further increases with brushing time led to significant efficacy increases.
  • Manual TB removed ~75% plaque in 90 seconds per quadrant; ETB removed 92–94% in the same time.

6.7Interdental Cleaning

  • Lang et al. (1973): plaque occurred first at interproximal areas of premolars/molars, then interproximal areas of anterior teeth, before any other surfaces.
  • TB alone = ineffective around the central part of the interdental space (Waerhaug 1981a); therefore, additional methods are required to access interproximal spaces.
  • Range of products able to penetrate between adjacent teeth: floss, woodsticks, interdental brushes, single tufted brushes.
  • Interdental brushes = soft nylon filaments twisted into a fine stainless-steel wire; conical or tapered shape.

6.8Interdental Cleaning: Floss

  • Demand for manual dexterity can be difficult and lead to poor compliance.
  • Used commonly in young adults and children, where papillae fill out interdental spaces.

Waerhaug (1981)

  • Floss could penetrate 2 mm or more below the tip of the papilla, but never more than 3.5 mm, without causing discomfort.
  • Where floss was used, adhering JE cells were missing, but new epithelial cells reappeared after 3 days, repopulating to baseline by 2 weeks → disruption to JE by floss is a more physiological event than a pathological condition.
Practical Tip Daily floss use is safe, but it is needless to force floss as deeply as possible — there is simply no plaque there to remove.

Graves et al. (1989)

  • 2-week clinical trial testing 3 types of dental floss (waxed, nonwaxed, dental tape) vs toothbrushing alone in reducing interdental bleeding.
  • Floss/tape group: mean interproximal bleeding reduction of ~70%, vs ~35% in the brushing-only group.
  • No difference in effectiveness between floss types.
  • Flossing reduced gingival bleeding interproximally substantially more than brushing alone.

Gjermo and Flotra (1970) — Floss vs Toothpicks (+ single tufted brush)

  • In young people, dental floss had greater plaque-removing effects than toothpicks, but took twice as long (4 mins).
  • Toothpicks + single tufted brush were found to be as effective as floss in removing interproximal plaque and took equally as long.

6.9Interdental Cleaning: Interdental Brushes (IDB)

  • Useful in areas where papilla may not fully fill out the interproximal region — consistently better rate of plaque removal than flossing.

Christou et al. (1998) and Jackson et al. (2006)

Key Finding Interdental brushes were more effective than dental floss in the removal of plaque and in pocket reduction in patients with moderate to severe chronic periodontitis.

Slots et al. (2008) — Systematic Review

Effectiveness of IDB as an adjunct to toothbrushing in plaque control and clinical parameters of periodontal inflammation, vs toothbrushing alone vs toothbrushing + floss/woodsticks:

  • In young patients, where papilla fills out interdental spaces, dental floss is the only tool to reach the area.
  • As an adjunct to brushing, IDB removes more plaque than brushing alone — inconclusive evidence of effect on gingival inflammation.
  • IDB removed more plaque than floss and woodsticks, but no difference on parameters of gingival inflammation.
  • More pocket reduction with IDB than floss.

6.10Conclusion

  • Periodontal disease is largely plaque-induced.
  • Instruction and enforcing OH plays a crucial role as a dental clinician.
  • 2× daily toothbrushing with electric or manual TB.
  • Supplemented with interproximal cleaning aids.
  • Apply preventive treatment protocols.
Final Principle The dental clinician should have an adequate understanding of the tools available to implement customized instructions per individual requirements.

7.1Rationale

Core Principle Plaque is the primary aetiological factor for gingival inflammation (Löe et al. 1965). Chemical plaque control is suggested as an adjunct to mechanical plaque removal — never a substitute.
  • Gingivitis and periodontitis lie on a continuum of the same inflammatory disease (Kinane et al. 2005).
  • Mechanical removal is effective but does not remove all plaque deposits — failure to comply, physical difficulties, and generally not-effective brushing technique all contribute.
  • <50% of plaque is removed after a single episode of brushing (De la Rosa et al. 1979).
  • Chemical agents support primary prevention of periodontitis and secondary prevention of recurrent periodontitis (Chapple et al. 2015 — EFP XI European Workshop in Periodontology, Prevention Workshop).

7.2Mechanisms of Action (Lang & Lindhe 2008)

Chemical agents act at four distinct points along the plaque maturation continuum (early/simple/G+ aerobic → late/complex/G− anaerobic):

1. Anti-adhesive

Prevent initial bacterial adhesion to the acquired pellicle. Earliest point of intervention.

2. Antimicrobial

Bacteriostatic or bactericidal action against early colonisers in the developing biofilm.

3. Alter Pathogenicity

Modulate the metabolism and virulence of established plaque without necessarily killing bacteria.

4. Plaque Removal

Disrupt and detach mature biofilm. Latest point — overlaps with mechanical removal.

Key Definitions (Lindhe et al. 1997)

Four formal categories distinguish what a chemical agent does to plaque and to clinical outcomes:

Antimicrobial Agents

Chemicals that have a bacteriostatic or bactericidal effect in vitro that alone cannot be extrapolated to a proven efficacy in vivo against plaque.

Plaque-Reducing / Inhibitory Agents

Chemicals that have only been shown to reduce the quantity and/or affect the quality of plaque, which may or may not be sufficient to influence gingivitis and/or caries.

Anti-plaque Agents

Chemicals that have an effect on plaque sufficient to benefit gingivitis and/or caries.

Anti-gingivitis Agents

Chemicals that reduce gingival inflammation without necessarily influencing bacterial plaque (includes anti-inflammatory agents).

Why the distinction matters A compound shown to be antimicrobial in vitro is not automatically an anti-plaque or anti-gingivitis agent in vivo — each requires its own clinical evidence.

Worked Example — NaF ppm calculation

For a toothpaste declaring 0.32% NaF:

  • Atomic mass: F = 19; NaF = 42 → %F in NaF = 19 / 42 = 45.24%
  • 0.32% NaF = 3.2 mg/g
  • 3.2 × 0.4524 = 1.45 mg/g F = 1,450 ppm F

7.4Chlorhexidine (CHX) — the Gold Standard

Chemistry & Mode of Action (Jones 1997)

  • Cationic bisbiguanide with broad antimicrobial and anti-plaque activity.
  • Targets the inner cytoplasmic membrane; affects bacterial metabolism.
  • Effective against Gram +ve, Gram −ve bacteria, yeasts, dermatophytes, and lipophilic viruses.
  • Low mammalian toxicity. Strong affinity for epithelial surfaces.
  • Bacterial cell surface is typically negatively charged; CHX is attracted to phosphate-containing compounds → integrity of cell membrane disrupted → increased permeability and leakage of low-molecular-weight components (e.g. ions).
  • Bacteriostatic at low concentrations; bactericidal at high concentrations. Irreversible damage = coagulation/precipitation of cytoplasm with phosphate complexes (e.g. ATP, nucleic acids).

Antimicrobial Effects — Key Studies

Briner et al. 1986 — supragingival

15 mL of 0.15% CHX bd in gingivitis patients (n = 430 adults). Significant reduction of Actinomyces (85–97%); no detectable shift in microbial species; microflora returned to baseline 3 months post-experiment.

Wade & Addy 1989 — subgingival in vitro

n = 9 chronic periodontitis patients; 0.2% CHX. Majority of subgingival bacteria affected. Resistance: S. mitior, S. sanguis, Capnocytophaga. Possible reasons for limited in vivo subgingival action: GCF proteins inhibit CHX; poor penetration into calculus/cementum/dentine; limited reach in CT.

Anti-plaque Effects & Substantivity

  • Positive charge distributed over nitrogen atoms either side of the hexamethylene bridge → strong substantivity.
  • Roberts & Addy 1981: antibacterial activity observed for up to 5 hours after rinsing for 60 s with 10 mL of 0.2% CHX.
  • Jenkins et al. 1988: 0.2% CHX on enamel inserts (electron microscopy) — immediate bactericidal effect; prolonged bacteriostatic effect.
  • Tooth-bound CHX prevents plaque formation; overall mucosa-bound quantity reduces salivary bacterial challenge; aids post-operative healing (Jones 1997).

Optimal Dose & Efficacy

Optimal Dose (Jones 1997) 40 mg of chlorhexidine per dose — delivered as either 10 mL of 0.2% bd or 15 mL of 0.15% bd. Reductions in this dose = reductions in clinical effectiveness.
  • Berchier et al. 2010 — systematic review: 409 titles screened, 8 eligible, 7 used for meta-analysis (same plaque index). Small but significant difference in favour of 0.2% over 0.12%. Difficult to determine efficacy of gel formulations (penetration uncertain).

Side Effects

  • Löe et al. 1976 (2-year daily use, n = 120 students, 10 mL of 0.2% daily): no systemic side effects; minute shift in salivary microflora toward less-sensitive species (returned to control levels after cessation). Limited GIT absorption due to cationic structure. Stained teeth and increased supragingival calculus noted.
  • Mariotti & Rumpf 1999: reduced human gingival fibroblast proliferation in vitro; low in vivo toxicity attributed to low free-CHX concentration and poor epithelial absorption. Authors discouraged CHX peri-operatively, especially during regenerative and surgical procedures.
  • No reports of systemic toxicity from topical application or ingestion; no teratogenicity in animal models; accidental IV infusions documented with no serious consequences; hypersensitivity reports inconclusive (Lang & Lindhe 2008).

7.5Phenols / Essential Oils

Triclosan

  • Trichloro-2-hydroxyphenyl ether — aromatic hydrocarbon with hydroxyl group; non-ionic phenol.
  • Interferes with enzymatic function of target cells; adsorbs to lipid portion of cells and interferes with cell transport.
  • Used in toothpastes, mouth rinses, anti-perspirants, soaps.
  • No staining — due to non-ionic nature.
  • Jenkins et al. 1991: anti-plaque effects ≤ SLS, significantly inferior to CHX.
  • Usually formulated with zinc citrate or Gantrez™ (PVM/MA copolymer):
    • Gantrez™ ↑ adsorption of triclosan onto saliva-coated hydroxyapatite in vitro (Nabi et al. 1989).
    • Zinc citrate + triclosan exerts greater plaque inhibition on surfaces with moderate plaque compared to clean surfaces (Saxton et al. 1988): zinc citrate inhibits bacterial proliferation; triclosan greatest effect on plaque-free surfaces.

Listerine® (Essential Oils)

  • Concentrated hydrophobic liquids of volatile aromatic plant compounds: eucalyptol, menthol, and methyl salicylate.
  • Anti-gingivitis effect due to antioxidative activity of the essential oils (Firatli et al. 1994).
  • Walker et al. 1989: daily use for 6 months as an adjunct → ↓ total microflora and reduced anaerobic/facultative cell ratio; long-term use ↓ total supragingival plaque without detrimental shift in community.
  • Lamster et al. 1983: 6-month double-blind RCT (n = 145) — plaque score ↓ 20.8% vs vehicle, 22.2% vs H₂O; gingivitis ↓ 27.7% vs vehicle, 28.2% vs H₂O. Gordon et al. 1985 (n = 144, 9 mo) replicated.
  • Van Leeuwen et al. 2011 — systematic review (19/390 articles): CHX provided superior plaque control vs essential oils; no significant difference in reduction of gingival inflammation.
  • Poor oral substantivity (Eley 1999). Weak associations with oral cancer have been reported (McCullough & Farah 2008).

7.6Quaternary Ammonium Compounds (QAC)

  • Salts of quaternary ammonium cations (NR₄⁺). Anti-plaque and antimicrobial — disrupt cell membrane.
  • Examples: benzalkonium chloride, cetylpyridinium chloride (CPC).
  • 0.05% CPC readily adsorbed onto oral surfaces at oral pH.
  • Disappointing substantivity: 3–5 hours (cf. CHX). Possible reasons: rapid resorption; loss of activity when desorbed (monocationic structure, unlike CHX's bicationic structure).
  • May be overcome by increasing frequency of use → compliance concerns. Lozenge vehicles have been developed.
  • Evidence for anti-gingivitis effect equivocal, especially when used alongside toothbrush + toothpaste.
  • Vandekerckhove et al. 1995: compared CPC mouth rinse vs CPC lozenge vs CHX mouth rinse — most profound effects with CHX; no difference between CPC formulations.

7.7Enzymes (Lang & Lindhe 2008)

1. Plaque-Removing Enzymes

Disrupt the early plaque matrix. Examples: dextranase, mutanase. Abandoned due to poor substantivity and reports of mucosal erosion.

2. Host-Defence-Enhancing Enzymes

Catalyse conversion of fermentable carbohydrates → H₂O₂ → converts thiocyanate to hypothiocyanite, which interferes with oral bacterial metabolism. Examples: glucose oxidase, amyloglucosidase. Inconclusive anti-gingivitis results; no convincing long-term studies.

7.8Amine Alcohols

  • Anti-plaque with minimal antimicrobial effect — interfere with plaque matrix formation and bacterial adherence.
  • Octopinol was the first compound to demonstrate anti-plaque efficacy.
  • Withdrawn over concerns of toxicity.
  • Available overseas as a mouth rinse.
  • Available in Australia as a dental hygiene chew for canines.

7.9Natural Products

Sanguinarine

  • Extract of Sanguinaria canadensis (blood-root). Antimicrobial effect likely due to ammonium ions.
  • Retained in plaque for hours; poorly absorbed from GIT. Formulated into toothpaste and mouth rinses, often with zinc salts.
  • Efficacy difficult to evaluate. Mascarenhas et al. 2002: associations reported with oral precancerous lesions.

Propolis

  • Bee product consisting of wax and plant extracts (flavones, flavanones, flavanols).
  • Used in homeopathic therapy as antiseptic, anti-inflammatory, anti-mycotic, bacteriostatic agent.
  • Murray et al. 1997: clinical efficacy as mouth rinse not shown; not significantly better at inhibiting de novo plaque growth than control.

Oil Pulling

  • Swishing edible oil between interdental spaces for 10–15 minutes on an empty stomach.
  • Used in traditional Indian folk therapy (headache, migraine, diabetes, asthma; caries, oral malodour, bleeding gums, dryness, cracked lips; "strengthens teeth, gums, jaws").
  • Kensche et al. 2013: edible oils may modulate plaque surface to impede adherence (↑ hydrophobicity); no evidence of substantivity. Significant binding of edible oils onto plaque is unlikely given the highly selective process of plaque formation.
  • Explanation for the effects of lipids is theoretical rather than evidence-based.

7.10Metal Salts

Inhibit plaque formation by interfering with bacterial glycolysis. Research results are mostly inconclusive.

7.11Other Agents

Povidone-Iodine (PVP-I)

  • Broad-spectrum antiseptic; used to treat and prevent wound infections.
  • Disappointing substantivity (≈1 hour with 1%) and disappointing plaque inhibitory effects.
  • Concerns with absorption through oral mucosa in iodine-sensitised patients (Eley 1999).

Sensitive-Toothpaste Chemicals (Walters et al. 2005)

Two main mechanism types:

  1. Potassium-containing toothpastes — depolarising effect on nerve conduction.
  2. Tubule-blocking toothpastes — strontium chloride, stannous fluoride, calcium sodium phosphosilicate, arginine — block exposed dentine tubules and interrupt external stimulation to achieve a desensitising effect.

7.12Conclusion

Take-Home (Chapple et al. 2015) The mechanical disruption of dental plaque is the gold standard for the prevention of plaque-related diseases. Chemical agents are recommended to support mechanical biofilm removal and when additional improvement in a patient's plaque control is required.

Practical Hierarchy of Evidence

  • Chlorhexidine remains the gold-standard chemical agent — best substantivity and broadest spectrum — but staining/taste effects limit long-term use.
  • Essential oils (Listerine) provide a useful long-term alternative with no staining.
  • Triclosan-containing dentifrices (with zinc citrate or Gantrez™) are convenient daily-use options.
  • CPC, stannous fluoride, sanguinarine, propolis, oil pulling — inferior to CHX or with insufficient evidence.
  • No chemical agent replaces toothbrushing and interdental cleaning.
