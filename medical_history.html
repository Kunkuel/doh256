Medical History for Dentists

Dr Michelle Kang Oral Medicine Specialist BDS (Otago), DClinDent (Oral Med), MRACDS
Core Principle
Think of the 4 critical processes for every organ system when reviewing a patient's medical history: haemostasis → wound healing → circulation → respiration. Apply this to extractions (the most invasive routine dental procedure).
01

Why Take a Medical History?

The most invasive thing we regularly do in dentistry is a dental extraction — it creates a hole in bone. After an extraction, 4 things must happen for safe recovery:

Process 1
Stop Bleeding

Haemostasis — the clot must form to prevent haemorrhage

Process 2
Wound Healing

Tissue must be able to repair; compromised in diabetes, immune disorders, etc.

Process 3
Blood Goes 'Around and Around'

Circulation — adequate perfusion to deliver healing factors to the site

Process 4
Air Goes 'In and Out'

Respiration — especially under sedation or in patients with respiratory disease

Also Consider
Oral manifestations of systemic disease · Medico-legal documentation · Building patient rapport
02

How to Take a Medical History

Different clinicians may approach the history differently, but it must always be:

Comprehensive

Cover all body systems — don't assume a condition is irrelevant

Systematic

Use headings/checklists so nothing is missed. Headings also act as a 'cue' for patients

Reproducible

Another clinician should be able to replicate and reach the same conclusions

Practical Tip
Record both YES and NO for each system. Recording negatives confirms conditions have been actively excluded, not overlooked. The form headings also serve as memory cues for patients who may not volunteer information unprompted.

Patient Observation on Entry

Before asking a single question, observe:

  • Gait — how they walk
  • Frailty — overall fragility/wellness
  • Mobility — use of aids, assistive devices
  • Who accompanied them — carer, parent, family
  • Mode of transport — car, public, community transport (relevant for sedation discharge planning)
03

Patient History Structure

A full patient history consists of five components:

  1. Reason for Visit/Attendance (RFV / RFA) — also called Chief Complaint (CC). Why is the patient here? What are their expectations?
  2. History of Presenting Complaint (HPC) — expand on the CC. Use structured pain assessment tools.
  3. Medical History (MHx) — systematic review of all body systems.
  4. Dental History (DHx) — previous dental experiences, attitudes, last visit, past treatment.
  5. Social History (SHx) — smoking, alcohol, recreational drugs, occupation, lifestyle.

Allergies & Adverse Drug Reactions (ADRs)

When documenting allergies, always record both: (1) name of drug and (2) type of reaction.

e.g. Codeine → constipation  |  Latex → anaphylaxis

Reference
Read the 'Allergies' section in Oral and Dental Therapeutic Guidelines v4 (2025), p236–239 for management of allergic reactions in dental practice.
Type A vs Type B ADRs
TypeDescriptionFrequencyExamples
Type A Predictable — based on known pharmacological actions of the drug 85–90% of all ADRs Overdose, side effects, secondary effects, drug interactions
Type B Hypersensitivity reactions — signs differ from pharmacological action 10–15% of all ADRs Intolerance, allergy, idiosyncrasy

Medications

Key Rule
Always document the drug name (active ingredient), not the brand name. If unknown, look it up in MIMS or the Australian Medical Handbook.

Record dosage and frequency — e.g. warfarin 5mg daily. Always cross-check medications against reported medical conditions (patients may 'forget' to mention conditions but the medication reveals them).

Drug Name vs Brand Name
TermDefinitionExample
Active ingredient (drug name)The chemical name of the compound that makes the medicine workParacetamol
Brand nameCommercially assigned name by the pharmaceutical companyPanadol, Tylenol
MRONJ — Critical Medication Alert
⚠ High Risk
Antiresorptive drugs (bisphosphonates, denosumab/Prolia) cause Medication-Related Osteonecrosis of the Jaws (MRONJ). Always ask about a history of these drugs before any invasive dental procedure.
  • Example: 6-monthly denosumab (Prolia) injections for osteoporosis — patient may not link this to dentistry
  • MRONJ risk persists even after stopping the drug
StageFeatures
Stage 0No necrotic bone visible; non-specific symptoms (pain, loosening)
Stage 1Exposed/necrotic bone, asymptomatic, no infection
Stage 2Exposed/necrotic bone + infection (pain, erythema, ± pus)
Stage 3Stage 2 + pathological fracture / extraoral fistula / OAC / osteolysis to inferior border

Supplements & Hospitalisations

Dietary Supplements

Natural products used to supplement the diet (vitamins, minerals, amino acids, enzymes, plant extracts, algae, fungi). Key points:

  • Manufacturers are not required to submit safety/effectiveness documentation to the Australian Register of Therapeutic Goods (ARTG)
  • Many potential harms: adverse effects, drug interactions
  • Must be recorded in the medical history — patients often do not volunteer this information

Read: Moses, G. (2021). The safety of commonly used vitamins and minerals. Australian Prescriber, 44(4), 119.

Hospitalisations

"Is my patient medically well?"

  • Recent/recurrent hospital admissions — any precautions after discharge? (e.g. recent MI → can I perform dental treatment?)
  • Any procedures? — Surgery: any complications? (bleeding, healing, circulation, respiratory)
Reference
For post-hospitalisation dental considerations, refer to Oral and Dental Therapeutic Guidelines v4 (2025).

Cardiovascular Disease (CVD)

CVD affects more than 4 million Australians and causes 1 in 4 deaths. Kills approximately 118 people per day — one person every 12 minutes.

Risk Factors
Behavioural
Modifiable Lifestyle
  • Unhealthy diet
  • Physical inactivity
  • Tobacco use
  • Excessive alcohol use
Intermediate Effects
Physiological Findings
  • Hypertension
  • Raised blood glucose
  • Raised blood lipids
  • Obesity
Outcomes
Major Adverse Events
  • Myocardial infarction
  • Heart failure
  • Stroke
  • Atrial fibrillation
Coronary Ischaemic Syndromes

Includes: stable angina and acute coronary syndromes (STEMI, NSTEMI).

Typical symptoms of acute coronary syndrome:

  • Crushing/heavy central chest pain → may radiate to arms, neck, back, jaw
  • Shortness of breath
  • Nausea
  • Sweating
⚠ Important
Some patients may present with no pain — especially older patients, females, and those with diabetes.
Dental Deferrals After Cardiac Events
PeriodRiskManagement
Within 12 months of MI / stent / CABG / TAVI Increased risk of major adverse cardiac event (incl. sudden death) Defer elective treatment for 6 months; emergency pain/infection → treat; consider specialist advice
Check antithrombotic use Bleeding risk from antiplatelet / anticoagulant therapy See haematology section — do not stop without consulting managing clinician
Reference
Read: 'Dental considerations for patients with a medical condition' in Oral and Dental Therapeutic Guidelines v4 (2025).
Hypertension & Dental Practice
  • Stress of dental treatment can further elevate BP
  • Major risk outcomes: atrial fibrillation, stroke, MI, end-stage kidney disease
  • Dental appointment stress → potential hypertensive crisis in uncontrolled patients
  • Measure BP at the start of appointments for at-risk patients

Infective Endocarditis (IE)

Infection of the endocardium — most commonly involving heart valves and intracardiac foreign bodies (e.g. prosthetic valves). Results in infected vegetations (microbial colonisation/invasion). In-hospital mortality: 15–20%.

Australian Incidence
5 cases per 100,000 person-years. High morbidity and mortality despite treatment.
Antibiotic Prophylaxis

Antibiotic prophylaxis against IE is recommended only for a restricted group of patients (Antibiotic Expert Groups). No RCT has established definitive benefit, but guidelines continue prophylaxis in high-risk cases.

Cardiac conditions requiring prophylaxis:

  • Prosthetic cardiac valve (including transcatheter-implanted/homograft)
  • Prosthetic material used for cardiac valve repair (annuloplasty rings and chords)
  • Previous infective endocarditis
  • Ventricular assist devices
  • Congenital heart disease — only if unrepaired cyanotic defects (incl. palliative shunts/conduits) OR repaired defects with residual defects at/adjacent to prosthetic patch (which inhibit endothelialisation)
  • Rheumatic heart disease

Dental procedures requiring prophylaxis (for above patients):

  • Procedures involving manipulation of gingival or periapical tissue or perforation of the oral mucosa
  • Extractions, implant placement, biopsy, soft tissue/bone removal, scaling (supra- and subgingival debridement), replanting avulsed teeth
Reference
Read: 'Prevention of infective endocarditis' within 'Antibiotic prophylaxis for dental procedures' in Oral and Dental Therapeutic Guidelines v4 (2025).

Haematology & Bleeding Disorders

Haemostasis — 3 Phases
  1. Vasoconstriction — initial response to vessel injury; reduces blood flow
  2. Platelet plug formation — platelets adhere, activate, and aggregate at the injury site
  3. Fibrin mesh formation — coagulation cascade produces fibrin to stabilise the platelet plug
Antithrombotic Drug Classes
ClassExamplesMechanism
Oral anticoagulants (DOACs)Apixaban, Dabigatran, RivaroxabanFactor Xa or thrombin inhibition
Oral anticoagulants (vitamin K antagonists)WarfarinInhibits vitamin K–dependent coagulation factors (II, VII, IX, X)
Injectable anticoagulantsHeparin, EnoxaparinAntithrombin activation → inhibits thrombin and Xa
Antiplatelet drugsAspirin, Clopidogrel, TicagrelorInhibit platelet activation/aggregation
Key Principle
The consequences of a thromboembolic event are usually more significant than dental bleeding. Do not interrupt antithrombotic therapy without consulting the managing clinician.
Risk Assessment — 4 Factors
  1. Properties of the antithrombotic drug (half-life, reversibility)
  2. Patient-related bleeding risk factors (e.g. renal/hepatic impairment, age, other drugs)
  3. Procedure-related bleeding risk (extraction > simple scaling)
  4. Likely clinical effect if bleeding or thrombosis occurs
Identifying a Potential Bleeder
  1. Comprehensive history (medications, family history, previous bleeding)
  2. Comprehensive examination
  3. Screening laboratory tests (INR, platelet count, APTT)
  4. Observation of excessive bleeding after a procedure

Respiratory System — Asthma

Asthma is a common chronic heterogeneous condition involving dysfunction of airway epithelium, smooth muscle, immune cells, and neuronal elements → airflow obstruction.

Airflow Obstruction Mechanisms
  • Bronchial smooth muscle spasm (bronchoconstriction)
  • Inflammation of bronchial mucosa
  • Mucus hypersecretion
  • Sputum plugging
Types of Asthma Triggers
Extrinsic

Allergic/atopic — e.g. dust mites, pollen, pet dander

Intrinsic

Idiosyncratic, non-allergic — e.g. cold air, exercise

Drug-Induced

NSAIDs (aspirin, ibuprofen), beta-blockers

Exercise-Induced

Triggered by physical exertion, especially cold/dry air

Dental Considerations for Asthma Patients
  • Take thorough history: precipitating factors, medications used, frequency/severity of attacks
  • Risk assessment: is asthma controlled? When was last hospital admission?
  • Instruct patient to bring their inhaler to every appointment
  • Avoid precipitating odorants (e.g. acrylics, glutaraldehyde) and drugs (NSAIDs if aspirin-sensitive)

CNS — Epilepsy

Critical History Points for Epilepsy
  • Type of seizures
  • Frequency of seizures
  • Duration per episode
  • Whether under GP/neurologist care
  • Any warning signs (aura) before an episode
Seizure in the Dental Chair — Management Steps
  1. Stop treatment immediately; move dental instruments away from the patient
  2. Assist patient to the floor safely
  3. Maintain the airway
  4. Call 000

Endocrine System — Diabetes

A group of metabolic diseases characterised by hyperglycaemia due to an inability to produce and/or use insulin, leading to metabolic and vascular complications.

Goals of Diabetes Therapy
  • Eliminate symptoms of hyperglycaemia
  • Reduce/eliminate long-term vascular complications (retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy, cardiovascular)
  • Allow the patient to achieve a normal lifestyle
Dental History Points for Diabetic Patients
  • Type of diabetes (Type 1 vs 2) and medications (insulin, metformin, etc.)
  • Managed by GP or endocrinologist?
  • Controlled or unstable? Last HbA1c?
  • Frequency of blood sugar monitoring
  • Know how to manage hypoglycaemia — have glucose available
Dental Relevance
Uncontrolled diabetes → impaired wound healing, increased infection risk, periodontal disease progression. Poorly controlled diabetes is associated with more severe periodontitis.

Musculoskeletal — Osteoporosis & MRONJ

Osteoporosis

Low bone mass and deterioration of bone tissue → increased fracture risk. Caused by: increased osteoclast activity + decreased osteoblast formation.

TypeCause
PrimaryNormal ageing; post-menopausal (oestrogen loss)
SecondaryMedical conditions/medications (hyperparathyroidism, long-term corticosteroids)
IdiopathicUnknown aetiology
MRONJ Definition & Criteria

All 3 criteria must be present for MRONJ diagnosis:

  1. Current or previous antiresorptive or antiangiogenic therapy
  2. Exposed bone (or bone that can be probed through a fistula) in the maxillofacial region persisting for >8 weeks
  3. No history of radiation to the jaws and no obvious metastatic disease to the jaws

Common causative drugs: Bisphosphonates (e.g. alendronate, zoledronic acid) and Denosumab (Prolia).

Joint Replacements

Whether antibiotic prophylaxis is indicated requires individual risk assessment. Consult the relevant section of Therapeutic Guidelines.

GI & Hepatic System

GORD (Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease)

Affects 10–20% of the developed world. Features: heartburn, regurgitation of food/acid, laryngopharyngeal reflux.

Dental Significance
Regurgitation of gastric acid into the oral cavity causes dental erosion — particularly on palatal surfaces of upper teeth. Must be factored into preventive and restorative treatment planning.
Liver Disease — Key Functions Relevant to Dentistry
  • Synthesis of all coagulation factors → haemostasis risk
  • Synthesis of albumin → drug transport/protein binding
  • Major site of drug metabolism → LA and analgesic dosing concerns
  • Immunological function (80–90% of innate immune proteins)
End-Stage Liver Disease
Risk of excessive bleeding (reduced coagulation factors, thrombocytopenia). May need vitamin K, platelet transfusion, or fresh frozen plasma before dental procedures.

Common dental drugs that need caution in liver disease: local anaesthetics · paracetamol · ibuprofen · antibiotics — dose reduction or avoidance may be needed.

Cancer & Social History

Carcinogenesis — 3 Stages
  1. Initiation — unrepaired DNA damage completes cell division → initiated daughter cells
  2. Promotion — clonal expansion of initiated cells (influenced by hormones, growth factors, promotors)
  3. Progression — development of malignant subclones; some become invasive and metastatic
Social History — Quantifying Risk

ISBAR Communication Framework

Used to communicate about a patient's condition clearly and safely — especially in urgent referrals or escalations.

I
Identify
Identify yourself (name, position, location) and who you are talking to. Identify patient name, age, sex, and location.
S
Situation
State the purpose: "The reason I am calling is…" If urgent, say so — e.g. "This is urgent because the patient is unstable with a BP of 90."
B
Background
Tell the story — current problem, relevant history, examination findings, test results, current management.
A
Assessment
State what you think is going on — e.g. "The patient seems to be deteriorating, I think they may be bleeding."
R
Request
State your request — e.g. "I need help urgently, are you able to come?" or "I'd like your opinion on the most appropriate next step."
Take Home Messages
  1. Think of the 4 critical processes (haemostasis · wound healing · circulation · respiration) for every organ system in the medical history.
  2. Ensure your medical history is Comprehensive · Systematic · Reproducible.
  3. Always document drug name (active ingredient) + dosage + frequency; cross-check with stated conditions.
  4. Record both YES and NO for each system to confirm exclusion.
  5. Consider antiresorptive drug history every time before any invasive procedure — MRONJ risk.
