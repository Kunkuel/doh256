Extra-oral & Intra-oral Examination

Dr Michelle Kang Oral Medicine Specialist Soft-tissue examination
Core Principle
Know "normal" anatomy & function before detecting abnormality. Every EO/IO examination must be comprehensive, systematic and reproducible — the same way, every time.
01

Why Examine?

Function
Perceptual interface

The maxillofacial region is our main perceptual & communication interface with the outside world.

Protection
First line of defence

Against inhaled/ingested infectious or toxic agents.

Sensation
Special senses

Sight, smell & taste have sensory receptors in this region.

Screening
Cancer detection

Oral & oropharyngeal cancer detection and screening.

Oral manifestations of systemic disease
The oral cavity shares biological, molecular & histological features with other systems (upper GI, respiratory, skin) — so systemic disease can show up in the mouth. Farah et al., 2019

Extra-oral Observation & Skin

EO sequence: gait & asymmetry → skin/hair → cervical lymph nodes → TMJ → muscles of mastication.

As they walk in
General observation

Gait, frailty, mobility, who is attending with them, and mode of transport (car / public / community transport).

Inspection
Skin & hair

All visible skin (head, face, arms, hands, feet): asymmetry, growths, bleeding/bruising, and lesions (pigmented or ulcerated).

04

Swelling, Trismus & Red Flags

For both facial swelling/asymmetry and trismus, work through:

  • Why? (cause) — run a diagnostic sieve: infective? inflammatory? congenital? neoplastic?
  • Where? — which structure (skin / muscle / fat / bone / salivary gland)?
  • Any red flags against the 4 critical processes — blood "around and around", air "in and out"?
Is this a medical emergency?
Swelling: dyspnoea, stridor, cyanosis, or spreading infection. Trismus: spreading infection, or the patient cannot maintain liquids orally. Any of these → treat as an emergency.

Cervical Lymph Nodes

You must understand head & neck lymphatic drainage: the "collar" of head nodes drains into the deeper neck nodes, then into the thorax. Nodes are grouped into Levels I–VI (superior → inferior). Robbins et al., 2002

Lymphatic system
Primary lymphoid
Forms & matures lymphocytes

Where lymphocytes form & mature into T and B cells — thymus, red bone marrow.

Secondary lymphoid
Filters & activates

Filters/monitors extracellular tissue & activates lymphocytes — lymph nodes, spleen, tonsils.

Palpation

Submandibular nodes are superficial (palpable in health): ask the patient to lower the chin, then gently pull the soft tissues laterally across the inferior border of the mandible to capture the node against the bone. Submental nodes: move the submental tissues anteriorly.

Inflammatory vs Neoplastic node
Superficial healthy nodes are soft, moveable, non-tender. Inflammatory (e.g. odontogenic infection): enlarged & tender, but usually still soft & moveable. Neoplastic (e.g. metastatic SCC): enlarged, firm, non-tender, and may be fixed / non-moveable (extracapsular spread).

Muscles of Mastication & TMJ

Four main pairs of masticatory muscles — palpate for tenderness/hypertrophy:

Masseters Temporalis Medial pterygoids Lateral pterygoids

The TMJ articulates the mandible with the cranium/temporal bone — a synovial joint (two bones in a capsule forming a joint cavity). Nanci & Ten Cate, 2013

DC/TMD — Schiffman et al., 2014
An evidence-based descriptive diagnostic system for common TMD subtypes, with two axes: Axis I = clinical examination; Axis II = chronic-pain, disability & psychological questionnaire. Palpation pressures are calibrated (~1.0 kg for muscles & dynamic TMJ, ~0.5 kg for the TMJ lateral pole).
07

Lips

The lips' vermillion border with the skin is normally sharply demarcated and homogenous in colour & texture. Visually inspect and palpate for surface irregularities (ulceration, crusting) and colour irregularities.

Screening
Inspecting & palpating the lips is lip-cancer screening.

Oral Mucosa

Skin and oral mucosa are the specialised organ covering the external body surface (skin) and lining the alimentary tract (mucous membrane). Know normal mucosa before interpreting mucosal disease.

Functions

Mechanical protection (physical compressive/shear, chemical & radiation injury, fluid loss) · barrier against microorganisms/toxins · immunological defence (humoral & cell-mediated) · exocrine (salivary glands — lubrication, digestion, buffering) · sensory (touch, proprioception, pain, taste).

Basic structure

Epithelium over connective tissue (lamina propria + submucosa); the two interdigitate via epithelial ridges/pegs. On skin the equivalents are the epidermis (epithelium) and dermis (lamina propria).

Three types of oral mucosa

The type at any site reflects its functional demand:

TypeFeaturesLocation
MasticatoryHigh compression/shear/friction; keratinised, thick lamina propria; binds to mucoperiosteum (except lateral hard palate)Gingiva (attached & free), hard palate

Ulcer vs Erosion
Ulcer = loss of full epithelial thickness. Erosion = partial loss of epithelium.
When you see something — STOP and think
Is it normal, a variation of normal, or pathology? → describe it → ask what? and why? → run the diagnostic sieve. Always know normal before calling something abnormal.
