1Occlusion & Malocclusion

Occlusion — definitions

GPT-9 (Glossary of Prosthodontic Terms, 9th ed.)
"The static relationship between the incising or masticating surfaces of the maxillary or mandibular teeth or tooth analogues."
Christensen (1905)
Preferred the simpler word "bite" — a general term for all forms of contact in which teeth meet.
Davies & Gray (2001)
Simply, "the contact between teeth."

Malocclusion — definitions

Dental Practice Board
An abnormal occlusion in which teeth are not in a normal position relative to adjacent teeth in the same jaw and/or the opposing teeth when the jaws are closed.
Orthodontists
"An appreciable deviation from ideal occlusion."
Medline (US NLM / NIH)
"The teeth are not aligned correctly."

2Centric Relation vs Centric Occlusion

The one-line distinction CR is a bone relationship (mandible → skull, based on condyle position). CO is a tooth relationship (mandible → maxilla, maximum tooth contact). CR is independent of the teeth; CO is independent of the condyles.

3Ideal Occlusion & Andrew's Six Keys

Ideal occlusion

  • Point contacts of the maxillary posterior lingual cusp tips and the mandibular posterior buccal cusp tips into the opposing central fossae or marginal ridges.
  • Forces on posterior teeth directed through the long axis of the teeth.
  • "Normal" buccal positioning: maxillary buccal cusps sit buccal to (outside) the mandibular teeth.

Andrew's Six Keys of Occlusion (1979)

  1. Molar relationship — the distal surface of the disto-buccal cusp of the upper first permanent molar occludes with the mesial surface of the mesio-buccal cusp of the lower second permanent molar.
  2. Crown angulation (mesio-distal tip) — the gingival portion of each crown is distal to the incisal portion; varies with tooth type.
  3. Crown inclination (labio-/bucco-lingual torque) — anterior teeth inclined enough to prevent over-eruption; upper posterior lingual tip constant 3→5 and increased in molars; lower posterior lingual tip increases progressively canine→molar.
  4. No rotations.
  5. No spaces.
  6. Flat occlusal planes (no exaggerated curve of Spee).

4Requirements of a Stable Occlusion & Guidance

Five requirements of a stable occlusion

  1. Stable stops on all the teeth when the condyles are in Centric Relation (CR).
    • Point contacts are on the lingual cusp tips of maxillary posterior teeth, the buccal cusp tips of mandibular posterior teeth, central pits or marginal ridges on posterior teeth, the incisal edges of lower anteriors, and the lingual surfaces of upper anteriors.
  2. Anterior guidance in harmony with the border movements of the Envelope of Function.
  3. Disclusion (separation) of all posterior teeth in protrusive movements by the most anterior teeth (anterior guidance).
  4. Disclusion of all posterior teeth on the non-working (balancing) side — the side where the lower teeth move toward the tongue.
  5. Disclusion of all posterior teeth on the working side during excursions — the side where the lower teeth move toward the cheek — provided by canine guidance.
    • Canine guidance (mutually protected occlusion): the vertical & horizontal overlap of the canine teeth causes disengagement (disclusion) of the posterior teeth during lateral movement of the mandible — protecting the posteriors from damaging lateral loads.
    • Group function (GPT): multiple contact relations between maxillary & mandibular teeth in lateral movements on the working side, whereby simultaneous contact of several teeth acts as a group to distribute occlusal forces — considered a risk factor for restorative work.
    • Incisal guidance: the influence of the contacting surfaces of the anterior teeth on mandibular movement — i.e. the anterior teeth guiding protrusion (the mechanism behind requirement 3).

5Occlusal Curves & Arch Form

Curve of Spee (anteroposterior)

The antero-posterior curvature of the occlusal surfaces. It begins at the tip of the lower canine and follows the buccal cusp tips of all the mandibular posterior teeth to the anterior border of the ramus. An ideal curve, if continued, would pass through the condyles; it approximates part of a circle with a ~4-inch radius.

Curve of Wilson (mediolateral)

The medio-lateral curve contacting the buccal & lingual cusp tips on each side of the arch. It results from the inward inclination of the lower posteriors (lingual cusps lower than buccal) and outward inclination of the upper posteriors (buccal cusps higher than lingual). This design permits easy access to the occlusal table. Function: as the tongue lays food on the occlusal surfaces, the taller buccal cusps stop it passing the chewing position, keeping food on the midline.

Dental arch formation

The maxillary arch is larger than the mandibular arch, so the maxillary teeth overhang the mandibular teeth — permitting the overbite/overjet relationship.

6Overbite & Overjet

Overbite
The vertical relation of upper to lower teeth in centric occlusion. Enables disclusion of the posteriors during incision and increases anterior biting efficiency.
Overjet
The horizontal relation of upper to lower teeth in centric occlusion. Increases the duration of occlusal contact during protrusive & lateral movements in incising and mastication.

8Other Malocclusions & Clinical Features

Crossbite vs Open Bite

Don't confuse the two A crossbite is a reversed horizontal (transverse / antero-posterior) relationship — a tooth bites on the wrong (inner) side of its opponent. An open bite is an absent vertical contact — a gap where opposing teeth fail to meet. They are different problems and can occur together.
Anterior crossbite
One or more maxillary anterior teeth occlude lingual (palatal) to the mandibular anteriors — the lower incisors sit in front of the uppers (reverse overjet). May be purely dental (tooth tipping) or reflect a skeletal Class III tendency.
Posterior crossbite
The buccal cusps of the maxillary posterior teeth occlude lingual to the buccal cusps of the mandibular posterior teeth (the upper bites inside the lower). Usually a narrow maxilla; can be unilateral or bilateral and may force a functional mandibular shift.
Anterior open bite (AOB)
No vertical overlap/contact of the incisors while the posterior teeth are in occlusion. Causes: thumb/digit sucking, tongue thrust, prolonged dummy use, or a skeletal pattern (increased lower-face height).
Posterior open bite (POB)
One or more posterior teeth fail to meet the opposing teeth when the mouth is closed. Causes: lateral tongue thrust, failed/arrested eruption, or ankylosis of a tooth.

Other features

Spaces / diastemas
Gaps between teeth; the midline diastema between the maxillary central incisors is the classic example.
Loss of vertical dimension (collapsed occlusion)
As teeth wear/shorten they keep erupting (dento-alveolar compensation), so they maintain contact but the clinical crowns shorten. Restoring worn teeth may require regaining space — crown-lengthening surgery is one option to expose more crown height.
Smile line
Amount of tooth/gingiva shown on smiling — high (gummy) / medium / low.
Mouth breathing
Associated with lip traps / incompetent lips and malocclusion, breathing difficulty and sinus issues.
Midline shift
Central incisors not coincident with the facial midline — often not clinically significant on its own.
Tongue thrust
Linked with digit sucking and anterior open bite; the tongue prevents incisor eruption. Management aims to stop tongue protrusion.
