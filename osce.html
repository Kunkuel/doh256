1. OSCE workflow — your 8 steps

Don't freelance. Follow the same sequence every time. The examiner is watching for systematic and reproducible assessment.

1
Greet & confirm identity. Name, DOB, reason for attendance. Observe gait, frailty, who's with them, mode of transport — first hint at general health.
2
Chief complaint, in patient's own words. Then HPC using SOCRATES if pain.
3
Medical history — go through SALUD headings out loud. Yes/no with cues to elaborate. Allergies → Meds → Supplements → Hospitalisations → CVS → HTN → Haem → Resp → CNS → Endo → Infectious → MSK → Immune → GI → Uro-gen → Hepatic → Skin → Cancer → Pregnant?
4
Dental history, social history, family history, OH habits, diet. All risk factor data.
5
Review the materials provided. Odontogram, radiograph(s), periochart, BP/vitals. Don't just glance — call out what you see.
6
Special tests if appropriate. Cold/EPT, percussion, palpation, mobility, TTP, mention INR / HbA1c / BP if needed.
7
Problem list → diagnoses. Group everything significant: medical issues, periodontal status, caries, endodontic, missing teeth, social/behavioural. Use the SOAP structure when writing up.
8
Treatment plan in the 5 phases. Systemic → Acute → Disease Control → Definitive → Maintenance. Always in this order.

2. History taking

SOCRATES — for pain HPC

  • Site — where exactly?
  • Onset — when, gradual/sudden, progressive?
  • Character — sharp / dull / throbbing / aching?
  • Radiation — spread to ear, jaw, neck?
  • Associations — swelling, bad taste, fever?
  • Timing — constant / intermittent / nocturnal?
  • Exacerbating/Relieving — hot, cold, sweet, biting, lying down, analgesia?
  • Severity — 0–10.

The "4 critical processes" lens

For every system in the medical history, ask:

  1. Will it stop bleeding? (haemostasis)
  2. Will it heal? (wound healing)
  3. Does blood circulate? (around & around)
  4. Does air move? (in & out)

This makes your medical history dental-relevant, not just a recital.

3. Medical history by organ system — what matters dentally

SystemKey conditions / medsDental implication & action
CVS Valve replacement, congenital heart disease, previous IE, rheumatic heart disease, MI / stent / CABG, angina High risk Defer elective treatment 6 months after MI / stent / CABG / valve surgery. Consider antibiotic prophylaxis per TG for the restricted high-risk group (prosthetic valve, previous IE, certain congenital defects, cardiac transplant w/ valvulopathy).
Hypertension BP categories (normal <120/80; elevated 120–129/<80; stage 1 130–139/80–89; stage 2 ≥140/90) Caution Defer elective treatment if BP ≥180/120 (hypertensive crisis). Stress reduction, short appointments, avoid LA with high-dose adrenaline if uncontrolled.
Haematological / antithrombotics Warfarin, DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran), aspirin, clopidogrel, heparin Bleeding risk Warfarin → check INR within 24 h before invasive procedure; INR <4.0 generally safe for simple extractions (per TG). Do not stop anticoagulants without consulting the prescribing clinician — thromboembolic risk usually outweighs bleeding risk. Use local haemostatic measures.
Respiratory Asthma, COPD, OSA Bring inhaler to every visit. Avoid NSAIDs in aspirin-sensitive asthmatics. Avoid sulfite-containing LA if sulfite allergy. Upright chair position.
CNS Epilepsy, stroke, anxiety, depression Stroke/seizure protocol: stop tx, clear instruments, airway, 000. Ask about frequency, triggers, last episode.
Endocrine Diabetes (T1/T2), thyroid Modifier Ask: HbA1c, last reading, hypoglycaemic episodes, GP/endo, medications. Periodontitis ↔ diabetes is bidirectional. Morning appointments, ensure eaten + taken meds. Hypoglycaemia is the acute risk in the chair.
Infectious Hep B/C, HIV, TB Standard precautions for all. Hep C: bleeding tendency if cirrhosis, drug metabolism affected. HIV: CD4 count, viral load, opportunistic infections.
MSK / Osteoporosis Bisphosphonates (alendronate, zoledronic acid), denosumab/Prolia, joint replacement MRONJ risk Antiresorptive + antiangiogenic drugs. Risk increases with: IV route, duration, concurrent steroids, diabetes. Prevent extractions where possible; avoid implants in higher-risk patients. Joint replacement: routine prophylaxis NOT recommended now (per current TG) unless specific indication.
Hepatic Cirrhosis, fatty liver, viral hepatitis Impaired clotting, drug metabolism. Avoid/reduce paracetamol dose. Avoid metronidazole in alcoholics.
Allergies (ADR) Type A (predictable) vs Type B (hypersensitivity) Record drug name + type of reaction. Penicillin allergy → use clindamycin or clarithromycin per TG. Latex allergy → latex-free environment. Anaphylaxis vs intolerance — clarify always.
Red-flag drugs to memorise: warfarin (INR), DOACs (no easy reversal), bisphosphonates & denosumab (MRONJ), prednisolone >7.5 mg/day chronic (adrenal suppression, healing), methotrexate (immunosuppression), chemotherapy agents, immunosuppressants post-transplant, SSRIs (bleeding risk, bruxism).

4. ASA classification & vitals

ASADescriptionDental implication
IHealthy patient, no systemic diseaseRoutine care
IIMild systemic disease, no functional limitation (well-controlled HTN, mild DM, mild asthma, smoker)Routine care + standard precautions
IIISevere systemic disease, functional limitation (uncontrolled DM, prior MI >6mo, COPD on oxygen, stable angina)Medical consult often required; modify treatment
IVSevere systemic disease, constant threat to life (unstable angina, recent MI <6mo, severe COPD)Hospital setting only; emergency care
VMoribundNot applicable in routine dental setting
Take vitals. BP, pulse, RR. State them out loud. If BP ≥180/120 → defer elective. If pulse irregular → ask about AF (often on warfarin/DOAC).

5. Clinical examination — what to call out

Extra-oral

  • Symmetry, swellings, lymphadenopathy (submandibular, cervical)
  • TMJ — clicking, deviation, tenderness
  • Skin: lesions, scars
  • Lip competence

Intra-oral

  • Soft tissue screen (oral cancer): tongue, FOM, buccal mucosa, palate, tonsillar pillars
  • Odontogram: missing, restorations, caries, wear
  • Periochart: PPD, BOP, CAL, recession, mobility, furcation, suppuration, plaque score
  • Occlusion: Angle class, overjet, overbite, crossbite, wear facets
  • Special tests: cold (Endo-Frost), EPT, percussion, palpation, mobility, TTP

7. The 5 phases of treatment planning

Flow: Assessment → Diagnose → Stabilise → Relieve → Control → Restore → Maintain.

Phase 1 — Systemic

Goal: ensure patient is safe to treat. Stabilise medical conditions.

  • Liaise with GP (ISBAR — Identity, Situation, Background, Assessment, Request)
  • Request relevant tests: INR, HbA1c, BP, FBC if indicated
  • Determine ASA category and modify accordingly
  • Antibiotic prophylaxis decision per TG (restricted to high-risk cardiac group)
  • Smoking cessation brief intervention (5A's: Ask, Advise, Assess, Assist, Arrange)
  • Diet counselling, alcohol reduction if relevant
  • Document any modifications and verbal conversations

Phase 2 — Acute

Goal: relieve pain, swelling, infection, trauma.

  • Distinguish emergency (life-threatening / incapacitating) vs urgency (prompt but not immediate)
  • Pulpal debridement / pulpotomy / extirpation for irreversible pulpitis
  • Incision & drainage for fluctuant abscess
  • Antibiotics only if systemic involvement (fever, malaise, lymphadenopathy, cellulitis, immunocompromised) — local infection alone does not require antibiotics
  • Analgesia — paracetamol ± ibuprofen (first line); consider drug interactions (warfarin + NSAID risk)
  • Provisional restorations / temporary cementation for fractured teeth

Phase 3 — Disease Control

Goal: eradicate active disease; control modifiable risk factors before definitive treatment.

  • Caries control: OHI, dietary advice (frequency & quantity of refined carbs), fluoride (toothpaste 1000–1450 ppm baseline; 5000 ppm Rx if high risk; varnish 22 600 ppm professionally), provisional restorations of cavitated lesions, sealants on susceptible pits/fissures
  • Periodontal: supra- & subgingival scaling and root surface debridement (NOT aggressive root planing — Nakib 1982, Nyman 1988). Reassess at 6–8 weeks with re-probe and BOP
  • Endodontic stabilisation: complete pulpal therapy initiated in acute phase
  • Extract teeth with hopeless prognosis (McGuire & Nunn 1996)
  • Behavioural: continue smoking cessation, glycaemic control liaison, oral hygiene reinforcement
  • Salivary substitutes / xerostomia management

Phase 4 — Definitive / Reconstructive

Goal: final restorations, prosthetics, complex care.

  • Perio surgery / regeneration: for residual ≥6 mm pockets, persistent 4–5 mm pockets with inflammation, infrabony defects, furcation
  • Orthodontic treatment
  • Elective (non-acute) endodontics: definitive RCT, retreatment, apical microsurgery
  • Direct & indirect restorations: composite, amalgam, onlays, crowns
  • Pre-prosthetic surgery: ridge preservation, soft tissue grafts
  • Replacement of missing teeth: implant, FDP, RPD, no replacement, complete denture, implant-supported overdenture
  • Avoid implants in: smokers, uncontrolled DM, MRONJ-risk patients

Phase 5 — Maintenance / Supportive Periodontal Therapy (SPT)

Goal: long-term stability; prevent recurrence.

  • Recall intervals based on caries + perio risk:
    • Low risk: 12 months
    • Moderate: 6 months
    • High risk (active perio, smoker, DM): 3 months SPT initially, extend to 6 if stable
  • Re-examine BOP, PPD, CAL, radiographs (bitewings 2-yearly low risk, annually high risk; PAs as indicated)
  • Reinforce OH, dietary, smoking status at each visit
  • Ongoing liaison with GP for systemic conditions

8. Using eMIMs & Therapeutic Guidelines live in the exam

eMIMs — when to open it

  • Any medication you don't immediately recognise
  • To confirm active ingredient vs brand name
  • To check interactions with dental drugs (LA, NSAIDs, antibiotics)
  • To find class of drug (anticoagulant subtype, antihypertensive class)

Therapeutic Guidelines (Oral & Dental) — key chapters

  • Periprocedural bleeding risk (antithrombotics)
  • Antibiotic prophylaxis for IE — restricted patient group
  • Prescribing for dental infections — first-line amoxicillin; clindamycin if penicillin-allergic
  • Analgesics for dental pain
  • Patients on antiresorptives — MRONJ prevention

State out loud: "I would refer to Therapeutic Guidelines chapter on [X] to confirm" — examiners want to hear that you know where to look.

9. Common OSCE pitfalls

  1. Jumping to treatment before diagnosis. "If you cannot establish a diagnosis, do not perform irreversible treatment."
  2. Forgetting the systemic phase. Antibiotic prophylaxis decisions, INR, GP liaison — all live here.
  3. Stopping anticoagulants on your own. Never. Consult prescriber; thromboembolic risk > bleeding risk almost always.
  4. Prescribing antibiotics for localised pulpitis or chronic abscess. Wrong. Drainage / pulpal therapy is the treatment.
  5. Missing MRONJ risk in osteoporosis / cancer patients on antiresorptives.
  6. Vague risk assessment. Always state caries risk (low/mod/high) and perio stage & grade with rationale.
  7. Not naming your frameworks. Say "AAE 2013", "2017 World Workshop", "ICCMS", "Stefanac phases".
  8. Treating a tooth in isolation. Always consider it in the context of the whole mouth and the patient's medical, social, and financial situation.

5-phase treatment plan

🩺 Phase 1 — Systemic

  • BP recheck after rest in chair. If persistently >160/100, defer elective treatment and refer to GP. Today's elevated reading likely pain/anxiety-related; for acute pain management OK to proceed with caution.
  • Liaise with GP (ISBAR): confirm HbA1c, BP control, request guidance re denosumab MRONJ risk for extraction if needed. Document conversation.
  • MRONJ risk assessment: patient on denosumab for 4 years, low-dose (osteoporosis dose, not oncology dose). Risk of MRONJ for extraction estimated <1% but real. Per AAOMS 2014 / AAOMS 2022 update and TG: optimise OH, consider atraumatic extraction technique, primary closure, antibiotic cover. Do not interrupt denosumab without consulting prescribing GP/endocrinologist — drug holiday is controversial for osteoporosis-dose denosumab and rebound fracture risk is significant.
  • Antibiotic prophylaxis (IE): not indicated — no high-risk cardiac condition.
  • Penicillin allergy: document. If antibiotics needed, use clindamycin 300 mg 6-hourly OR metronidazole 400 mg 12-hourly per TG (avoid metronidazole if alcohol consumed).
  • Diabetes: well-controlled. Morning appointment, ensure eaten + taken metformin. Periodontitis-diabetes link discussed with patient.
  • Anxiety: reassurance, short appointments, possible nitrous oxide sedation; consider stress-reduction protocol.

🚨 Phase 2 — Acute

  • 26 pulpal debridement / extirpation under LA (articaine 4% with adrenaline 1:200 000 if BP controlled; otherwise 1:100 000 with aspirating syringe and limited dose). Place CaOH dressing, temporary restoration.
  • Analgesia: paracetamol 1 g QID (max 4 g/day) + ibuprofen 400 mg TDS — check no contraindication (no renal disease, no NSAID allergy, no GI ulcer). She is on perindopril → NSAIDs short course OK but monitor; avoid long-term.
  • Antibiotics not indicated — localised infection without systemic involvement. State this explicitly per TG.
  • Decision on definitive 26 management deferred — RCT vs extraction discussed at reassessment (see Phase 4 decision-making).

🦠 Phase 3 — Disease Control

  • Caries control protocol (HIGH risk):
    • OHI: modified Bass technique, twice daily; introduce interdental brushes (size appropriate) — demonstrate
    • Prescribe 5000 ppm sodium fluoride toothpaste (Neutrafluor 5000 Plus) daily, brush and spit don't rinse
    • Professional fluoride varnish (22 600 ppm) at scaling visits, 3-monthly initially
    • Dietary advice — critical: reduce sugar in coffee/tea (suggest gradual reduction or non-cariogenic sweetener); biscuits limited to mealtimes only (frequency > quantity); increase plain water intake
    • Xylitol gum after meals (stimulates saliva, reduces cariogenic bacteria)
    • Salivary substitutes if symptomatic xerostomia persists
    • Restore 27 mesial — composite, indirect pulp cap if close to pulp; ensure good marginal seal
  • Periodontal disease control:
    • Supra- and subgingival scaling and root surface debridement (not aggressive root planing) — full mouth, 2 quadrants/visit, under LA where needed
    • Site-specific debridement at 26 distal (pending decision on tooth)
    • Re-evaluate at 6–8 weeks: re-probe, BOP, plaque score
  • Reinforce OH at every visit; aim plaque score <20%, BOP <10% before moving to definitive phase.

🔧 Phase 4 — Definitive / Reconstructive

  • 26 decision — RCT vs extraction:
    • RCT + cuspal-coverage restoration (crown): avoids extraction (avoiding MRONJ risk); requires significant remaining tooth structure, post if needed; cost & multiple visits; perio-endo prognosis guarded due to 8 mm pocket DP. Per McGuire & Nunn 1996, prognosis is questionable to poor.
    • Extraction: resolves the problem but carries MRONJ risk on denosumab; atraumatic technique, primary closure, antibiotic cover (clindamycin given penicillin allergy), close follow-up at 1, 2, 4, 8 weeks.
    • Recommended: attempt RCT if structurally restorable and patient accepts guarded prognosis and cost. Discuss thoroughly, obtain informed consent. If unrestorable or perio-endo prognosis worsens — extract with MRONJ precautions.
  • Replacement of 26 (if extracted) and 36 (long-standing):
    • Implants — contraindicated given denosumab MRONJ risk
    • FDP (bridge): requires preparation of sound teeth; 36 space partially closed and 37 tilted — challenging
    • RPD (removable partial denture): cost-appropriate for pensioner; non-invasive; acceptable function
    • No replacement: acceptable if patient functional and aesthetic concerns are minimal
    • Decision per patient preference, prognosis of other teeth, cost.
  • Restore 27 if not done in disease control phase (definitive composite).
  • Replace failing/marginal older restorations as identified at reassessment.
  • Perio surgery only if residual ≥6 mm pockets after reassessment — unlikely given Stage I baseline; not anticipated.

♻️ Phase 5 — Maintenance / SPT

  • 3-monthly SPT initially (high caries risk + recent active perio + diabetic) for first 12 months, then reassess.
  • Extend to 6-monthly when: plaque score consistently <20%, BOP <10%, no new caries, dietary changes sustained.
  • Bitewings annually (high caries risk); PA of 26 at 6 months and 1 year post-treatment to monitor periapical healing.
  • Continue dietary & OH reinforcement, fluoride varnish.
  • Ongoing liaison with GP re HbA1c, BP, denosumab management.
  • Annual oral cancer screening.
  • If MRONJ symptoms develop (exposed bone, non-healing site, pain, paraesthesia) → immediate review and refer to oral surgery.

Why this case tests you well

  • MRONJ risk decision-making — the highest-yield drug interaction in modern dental treatment planning
  • Antibiotic stewardship — localised pulpitis/abscess does NOT need antibiotics; penicillin allergy alternative
  • Perio-endo lesion — distinguishing primary endo with secondary perio involvement and how it affects prognosis
  • Caries risk stratification with HIGH-risk interventions (5000 ppm, varnish, dietary)
  • Implant contraindication reasoning — not all missing teeth get implants
  • BP at elevated reading — when to defer, when to proceed with caution
  • Multidisciplinary liaison — GP, possibly endocrinologist, oral surgery referral pathway
