1Properties of X-Rays

X-rays are a form of electromagnetic radiation. Their key physical properties explain both how they form an image and why they are hazardous:

  • Invisible and weightless — cannot be detected by the senses.
  • Very short wavelength (≈ 0.01–0.05 nm) — high energy, highly penetrating.
  • Travel in straight lines from a point source and cannot be focused by a lens (this is why image geometry depends entirely on positioning).
  • Travel at the speed of light (3 × 10⁸ m/s).
  • Differentially absorbed by matter — dense tissues (enamel, bone, metal) absorb more → appear radiopaque (white); soft tissue/air absorb less → radiolucent (dark). This differential absorption is the image.
  • Can be deflected (scattered).
  • Cause certain substances to fluoresce (basis of intensifying screens / digital sensors).
  • Can ionise gases and produce biological changes in living tissue (the basis of radiation risk).

2The X-Ray Tube

X-rays are produced inside an evacuated tube with two electrodes — a cathode (negative) and an anode (positive):

  1. A current heats the tungsten filament at the cathode → electrons are "boiled off" (thermionic emission).
  2. A high voltage (kV) accelerates these electrons across the tube toward the anode target.
  3. The electrons strike the tungsten target plate on the anode. The collision converts their energy into X-ray photons (~1%) and heat (~99%).
  4. Photons are emitted through the tube window, pass through the patient's tissues, and strike the image receptor (film/sensor) behind the teeth.

3Image Types & Their Uses

Intra-oral (receptor inside the mouth)

TypeWhat it shows / Use
Periapical (PA)The whole tooth (crown + root) and the surrounding periapical bone. Usually 2–4 teeth per film. Root-focused.
Bitewing (BW)Patient bites on a "wing". Shows crowns + crestal bone of upper & lower teeth together — best for proximal caries and bone-level screening.
OcclusalLarger film held on the occlusal plane. Important for trauma / displaced fragments in soft tissue, unerupted teeth, salivary calculi.

Extra-oral (receptor outside the mouth)

TypeWhat it shows / Use
OPG (panoramic)All four quadrants in one image; tube + receptor rotate in a spiralling movement around the head. Workflow tip: count the teeth, then assess bone loss, then the teeth. Taken static, so patient angulation doesn't distort it the way intra-oral does — but lower resolution / fine detail than a bitewing.
CephalometricStandardised lateral skull view — orthodontic jaw & growth assessment.
Lateral obliqueView of the posterior mandible/ramus.

What we use X-rays for

  • Detect hard-tissue pathology: caries, periodontal bone loss, bony pathology, tooth fractures, bone fractures (apical third fractures are most common).
  • Detect subgingival calculus — small radiopaque "lumps" on the root surface.
  • Locate objects: foreign bodies, calculi, root tips, dental implants.
  • Evaluate growth & development: face/skull growth, eruption timing of deciduous & impacted teeth, asymmetries.

4Image Quality

Sharpness
How well a boundary between two areas of different radiodensity is revealed (a crisp vs blurred edge).
Resolution
How well the radiograph reveals small objects that are close together.
Magnification
The increase in image size compared with the true object size (a geometric distortion).
Contrast
The difference in density between image areas. High contrast = mostly black & white. Low contrast = many shades of grey (light grey vs dark grey).
OPG vs Bitewing An OPG gives more coverage but less resolution / fine detail than a bitewing. Because the OPG is captured while the machine is static, angulation does not distort it the way it does intra-oral images; but for detecting early proximal caries the bitewing remains superior.

5Radiation Dose, Risk & Biological Effects

Dose quantities

Exposure
A measure of radiation quantity — the capacity of radiation to ionise air.
Absorbed dose (Gy)
Energy absorbed per unit mass of matter. 1 Gy = 1 J/kg = 100 rad.
Equivalent dose, HT (Sv)
Compares the biological effect of different radiation types on a tissue/organ.
Effective dose, E (Sv)
Used to estimate overall risk in humans across all exposed tissues.

Linear Non-Threshold (LNT) hypothesis

LNT — the working assumption in dentistry There is a linear relationship between dose and the risk of inducing a new cancer, with no safe threshold — even small doses carry some risk. Therefore: minimise dose wherever possible.

Radiosensitive structures in the head & neck: thyroid gland, salivary glands, bone marrow (leukaemia risk), brain. Dental exposures have specifically been linked to meningiomas, salivary-gland tumours and thyroid tumours. As a scale of risk: roughly 1 excess cancer fatality per ~47,600 full-mouth examinations made with D-speed film and round collimation (Ludlow et al., 2008).

Biological effects — Deterministic vs Stochastic

Deterministic (tissue reactions)Stochastic
MechanismKilling of many cellsSublethal DNA damage
Threshold?Yes — occurs above a threshold dose; severity ↑ with doseNo threshold — probability ↑ with dose
ExamplesRadiation burns, cataractsCancer, hereditary mutations
Relevant to dentistry?No — doses are far too lowYes — the risk we manage with ALARA

6Protection, ALARA & Legislation

ALARA As Low As Reasonably Achievable. Every exposure must be justified (it changes management), optimised (lowest dose for an adequate image), and limited. The supervisor and the patient must both know the justification. Justification must be higher for children, who are more radiosensitive.

Legislation & responsibility

  • A licence is required to take radiographs.
  • The practitioner who records the radiograph is responsible for it.
  • Store radiographs for at least 7–10 years (longer for children).
Retakes Repeat an X-ray only when the diagnostic criteria are not met. Keep a record of all re-takes, stating the type of X-ray, the type of error, and the cause.

7Bitewing Radiography

Indications

  • Routine examination & detection of dental caries (proximal lesions).
  • A baseline record and follow-up of any suspicion or dietary change.
  • Investigate periodontal status — a 5 mm pocket or more (or 4 mm with bleeding) is a good reason to also take a periapical.

Ideal outcomes of a bitewing

  • Adequate image quality.
  • ~2 mm of root apical to the cemento-enamel junction (CEJ) must be visible (so crestal bone is assessable).
  • Crown overlap as minimal as possible.
  • Teeth captured from the distal of the lower canine posteriorly.
Trap — Cervical Burnout Cervical translucency ("burnout") is NOT caries. It is a radiolucent band at the CEJ caused by the thinner tissue there. Always confirm with clinical examination.

Technique methodology

  • The bite-platform sits on the middle of the film, parallel to its upper and lower edges.
  • Film horizontal for posterior bitewings, vertical for anterior (clinical indications can vary this).
  • Posterior teeth and film should be in contact (or as close as possible) and parallel.
  • Horizontal plane: aim the beam so it meets teeth & film at right angles and passes through the contact areas (avoids overlap).
  • Vertical plane: tube head angled downward 5°–8°.

Two positioning techniques

Tab technique
Tab attached to the film, tube head aligned by eye. Simple, cheap, disposable, usable in kids — but operator-dependent and cone-cutting is common.
Holder + beam-aiming device
Facilitates positioning & alignment. Less operator-dependent, reduces cone-cutting — but holders are not usually suitable for children.

Steps

Select film size → select holder/tab → position patient with occlusal plane horizontal and head supported → insert film gently (don't bend) → ask patient to close firmly → check film position while closing → expose.

8Periapical Radiography — Paralleling vs Bisecting

Periapical (PA) techniques show individual teeth and the tissues around the apices. Each film usually shows 2–4 teeth with detail of the teeth and surrounding alveolar bone.

Clinical indications

  • Detection of apical infection / inflammation; apical cysts & other bony lesions.
  • Assessment of periodontal status (especially deep pockets).
  • Post-trauma assessment of teeth & alveolar bone.
  • Presence / absence / position of an unerupted tooth; root morphology.
  • Post-operative implant evaluation.

Ideal positioning requirements

  • Tooth and film parallel to one another.
  • Film long axis vertical for incisors/canines, horizontal for premolars/molars.
  • Tooth under investigation + both neighbours visible.
  • ~2 mm beyond the apex visible.
  • Beam meets tooth & film at right angles (both planes); positioning reproducible; dot toward the occlusal/incisal aspect.

Paralleling technique

The film is held in a holder parallel to the long axis of the tooth; the cone is aimed at right angles to both tooth and film (vertically and horizontally). The holder makes it reproducible. Steps: select holder & film (smooth surface toward the tube) → position patient (head supported, occlusal plane horizontal) → patient bites gently to stabilise → cotton roll on the opposite side for comfort → slide the locator ring down to the face → align cone with the locator ring → "keep still, please" → expose. Aim the film toward the midline so the apex is captured while the film stays parallel; follow the arch as it curves. Always use paralleling whenever possible.

Bisecting-angle technique

Used when paralleling is not possible. The film is placed as close to the tooth as possible without bending; the angle between the tooth's long axis and the film's long axis is mentally bisected, and the tube head is positioned at right angles to that bisecting line. The occlusal plane should be horizontal. Requires patient cooperation. Can be done with a holder or by asking the patient to gently support the film with a finger/thumb.

Vertical angulation
The angle of the central ray to the occlusal plane. Too steep → foreshortening; too flat → elongation.
Horizontal angulation
Aim the beam through the interproximal contact area to avoid overlapping the teeth.
Bisecting — AdvantagesBisecting — Disadvantages

9Occlusal Radiography

Maxillary — Upper Standard (anterior) Occlusal

Indications: periapical assessment of upper anteriors in patients who can't tolerate PA holders; locate unerupted canines, odontomes, supernumeraries; the midline view for the parallax method (bucco-palatal position of maxillary canines); size/extent of cysts or tumours in the anterior maxilla; fractures of anterior teeth & alveolar bone. Positioning: patient upright, occlusal plane parallel to floor, head supported; dot toward the lip; thyroid collar; film centred & extended as posteriorly as possible; tube on the midline aiming down through the bridge of the nose at 60°–70° to the film. (An upper oblique occlusal also exists.)

Mandibular — Lower 90° (true) Occlusal

Indications: presence/position of radiopaque calculi in the submandibular ducts; bucco-lingual position of unerupted mandibular teeth; bucco-lingual expansion of the mandible by cysts/tumours/lesions; displaced fractures of the anterior mandible (horizontal plane).

Mandibular — Lower 45° (standard) Occlusal

Indications: periapical assessment of lower teeth (useful when PA holders aren't tolerated); size/extent of cysts/tumours in the anterior mandible; displaced anterior mandibular fractures (vertical plane).

Patient prep (all occlusals) Explain the technique · check for prior radiographs · ask about issues (e.g. pregnancy) · apply radiation-protection measures.

10SLOB & Advanced Techniques

Exam Q — SLOB Rule Same Lingual, Opposite Buccal. Take two radiographs with a horizontal shift of the tube head and compare how the object moves relative to a fixed reference (usually a tooth). It localises objects in the third dimension that a single 2D film cannot.
  • If the object moves the SAME direction as the tube shift → the object is LINGUAL / palatal.
  • If the object moves the OPPOSITE direction to the tube shift → the object is BUCCAL.

Used to localise impacted teeth, extra root canals (e.g. during RCT), and foreign objects. (Also called the tube-shift / parallax / Clark's rule.)

Other advanced techniques

Modified bisecting technique · Lip radiography (for fragments lodged in the vestibule — e.g. a child with a chipped tooth fragment in the lip). Think critically — when to modify technique: gag reflex, shallow palate, tooth tender to percussion / painful to bite on the holder, holder unavailable, or an uncooperative patient.

11Periodontal Charting (read alongside radiographs)

Core measurements Probing depth + recession = Clinical Attachment Loss (CAL). Radiographs confirm bone levels; charting confirms soft-tissue attachment.

Landmarks

  • Gum line → base of pocket = probing depth (PPD).
  • CEJ → gum line = recession. If there is no difference between CEJ and gum line → no recession (if you can see yellow root surface, recession is present).

6-point charting

Record six sites per tooth: distobuccal, mid-buccal, mesiobuccal and distolingual, mid-lingual, mesiolingual. Check bleeding on probing (BOP) at all six points — assessed after probing depth & recession are recorded.

Furcation involvement

Probe the furcation entrances: upper molars — mid-buccal, mesio-palatal, disto-palatal; lower molars — mid-buccal, mid-lingual. Graded Class I / II / III.

Mobility

Apply a horizontal force to each tooth and assess movement. Significant = > 1 mm. Graded 1 / 2 / 3.
